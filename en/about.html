Why I created this websiteW

Background

While switching jobs and frantically sending out resumes in February and March of this year (2025), I came across a company that provides CMS-based website building services. It suddenly reminded me of my previous multiple unsuccessful attempts to build a website. After a brief reflection, I realized the problem wasn’t at the code level—it was stuck in content planning and visual design. I tend to overthink before actually starting something. For instance: How many tags should I predefine? How should I categorize the directories? How can I maintain a consistent style? Do I need multilingual support? And I particularly dislike hassle. In the end, I often lose motivation before even getting started. So this time, after some soul-searching, I decided to focus on content above all—the priority was to make sure I could keep writing, and everything else would take a back seat. Of course, before finally settling on the tech stack and starting to write this article, I still went through a fairly long period of indecision.

And now, this is already the second version of this article.

Tech Stack Selection

After landing a job and having plenty of free time before starting the new position, I decided that “this time, I’m really going to build the website properly,” and began evaluating tech stack options.

The primary principle was that everything should serve the purpose of writing. This not only meant having a user-friendly editor but also required eliminating, as much as possible, all potential obstacles to writing—including laziness.

Looking back at my failed experiences with Next.js + Markdown and Next.js + GitHub issues, I realized that instead of manually managing documents, I needed to incorporate a CMS. When it comes to CMS, while I’m not highly experienced, I’ve dabbled with WordPress (PHP) and Wagtail (Python). However, as someone with a semi-developer background and influenced by my previous frontend work, support for MDX became a major consideration. This single requirement alone ruled out most frameworks. Taking into account lightweight design and cost, I eventually settled on TinaCMS. It has its flaws, but it’s serviceable.

Then came the frontend. Due to my familiarity with React and past experiences, my instinct was to choose a React-based solution. But honestly, the direction React has taken in recent years makes me a bit uneasy (I’ll discuss this in more detail another time). Although it was hard to let go of the React ecosystem, including its UI libraries, I ultimately decided on Astro for the frontend, considering that content is the priority for this website.

Of course, that was already the decision for the previous version.

Given Astro’s high degree of flexibility, the trade-off was that I had to handle more parts manually. To avoid writing various components and CSS from scratch, I eventually chose Next.js—which is what this current version is built with. This doesn’t mean I particularly like this framework; it’s simply because there aren’t better options in the React ecosystem for SSG (Static Site Generation).

Core Motivation

Aside from my interest in building websites, the fundamental driving force is that I genuinely need a platform for managing written content.

I enjoy learning—and I believe I like learning. When I learn, I need to take notes; otherwise, it’s as if I never learned. But I don’t like using note-taking platforms or software, and I dislike having scattered .txt or .doc files everywhere—organizing them is a huge hassle. As a result, I often skip taking notes, which makes it hard to even start learning. And not keeping a record of what I’ve learned inevitably leads to anxiety, which in turn makes it difficult to embark on new learning. It’s a vicious cycle.
