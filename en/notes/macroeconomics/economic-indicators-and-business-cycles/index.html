1. Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is the most commonly used indicator to measure the size of a country's economy.

Complete definition: The market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's geographical boundaries during a given period.

Each word in the definition has a specific meaning and is indispensable:

  • Market Value: Output is measured by price, not quantity. Producing an apple and a car contributes completely differently to GDP. Market prices reflect people's subjective valuation of the commodity and are the best proxy for measuring actual value.
  • Final Goods and Services: Only goods for final use are counted, not intermediate goods used in the production process. In the production chain of cotton → cotton yarn → cotton cloth → jeans, only the value of the final jeans is counted, not the value of each stage repeatedly accumulated—otherwise, it would lead to double counting.
  • Produced Within a Country: As long as it is produced within the country, regardless of the nationality of the producer or enterprise, it is included in that country's GDP. This differs from GNP (Gross National Product)—GNP is calculated by nationality, while GDP is calculated by geographical location.
  • In a Given Period: Usually measured annually or quarterly. The same commodity will not be counted repeatedly in different periods.

Situations not included in GDP:

Type Not IncludedReasonExample
Intermediate goodsTo avoid double countingSteel used to manufacture cars
Resale of used goodsProduction occurred in the past, already included in previous GDPSecond-hand housing transactions, used cars
Transfer paymentsNo new goods or services are producedSocial security benefits, pensions, unemployment benefits
Household productionNo market transactionsCooking for oneself, childcare
Illegal activitiesCannot be trackedUnderground economy

⚠️ Transfer Payment is a common point of confusion: Government paying pensions to retirees is not included in GDP; government hiring people to build roads is included in GDP. The distinction lies in whether new goods or services are produced.

1.1 Circular Flow Diagram

The circular flow model is built on two simplifying assumptions:

  1. Households own all factors of production
  2. Firms produce all goods and services

In reality, neither of these assumptions is entirely true—a large number of factors of production are held by firms, and households also produce some goods and services themselves (but these do not enter the market). The value of this model lies in revealing the logical structure, rather than precisely describing reality.

In this model, money and real flows move in opposite directions, forming a closed loop:

  • Real flow (clockwise): Factors of production flow from households to firms; goods and services flow from firms back to households.
  • Money flow (counter-clockwise): Consumption expenditure flows from households to firms; factor income (wages, rent, interest, profit) flows from firms back to households.

In this closed loop, because households are the sole providers of all factors of production and the sole purchasers of all goods and services:

  • Household expenditure = Firm revenue
  • Firm revenue = Firm costs (wages + rent + interest) + profit = Household income

⚠️ The second equality holds on the premise that "profits also belong to households"—that is, firms are owned by households, and profits ultimately flow back to households. This is a model assumption, not a universal fact.

Therefore, in this model: Total Expenditure = Total Income = GDP, and the following three methods of measuring GDP yield the same result.

1.2 Three Methods of Measuring GDP

The three methods observe the same economic activity from different perspectives, and the results are completely equivalent. The sum of the demand side (C+I+G+X-M) and the production side (durables/non-durables/services/construction/inventories) also yields exactly the same result—because every market transaction must have both a buyer and a seller, the total amount purchased must equal the total amount produced.

Method One: Expenditures Approach

The most commonly used method. Sums up all expenditures on final goods and services in the economy:

Y = C + I + G + (X - M)

Each component is detailed in 1.3.

Method Two: Income Approach

Sums up the income from all factors of production:

Y = w + i + r + p
SymbolMeaning
w (wages)Wages earned from labor
i (interest)Interest earned from capital
r (rent)Rent earned from land
p (profit)Profit earned from entrepreneurial ability

Method Three: Value-Added Approach

Sums up the value added at each stage of the production chain.

Taking jeans as an example:

StageMarket Value of OutputValue Added
Cotton farmer$10$10
Yarn factory$20$10
Fabric mill$30$10
Jeans factory$50$20
Total$50

Advantage of the value-added method: Applicable to cross-border production chains, it only calculates the value newly added within the country, avoiding double counting value already created in other countries.

Example: A product with components produced in China (value $30) and assembled into a finished product in the US (final price $50). Under the value-added method, China's GDP increases by $30, and the US GDP increases by $20 (only counting the value newly added within the US). If using the final product method, only the US $50 is included in US GDP, and China's $30 is not double-counted—the two methods yield consistent results, but the value-added method is clearer when tracking cross-border supply chains.

1.3 Components of the Expenditures Approach

C: Consumption

Household expenditure on final goods and services, excluding new home purchases.

Consumption is the largest component of GDP, accounting for about 68% in the US, and is relatively stable, not fluctuating significantly.

Includes: food, clothing, cars (for private use), medical care, education, entertainment, etc.

⚠️ Education expenditure in economic GDP accounting belongs to C (consumption), not investment—even though in everyday language we consider education an "investment." It's important to distinguish between economic definitions and everyday language definitions.

I: Investment

Business expenditure on capital goods, plus household new home purchases.

I = Business capital expenditure (equipment, software, plant)
  + Business inventory changes (Inventory)
  + Household new home purchases

⚠️ "Investment" in the economic sense ≠ "investment" in everyday language:

Everyday LanguageEconomic GDP Accounting
Buying stocksInvestmentNot included in GDP (just asset transfer)
Buying bondsInvestmentNot included in GDP
Firms buying equipmentInvestmentIncluded in I
Firms increasing inventory—Included in I
Households buying new homes—Included in I
Households buying used homes—Not included in GDP

Investment is the most volatile part of GDP, accounting for about 17% in the US. New technologies or products can drive surges in investment, but investment can contract sharply when confidence declines—this is one of the important reasons for cyclical fluctuations in GDP.

Intertemporal treatment of Inventory:

If the production chain is not completed within the accounting period (e.g., cotton yarn is still in the warehouse at year-end), the market value of this intermediate good is included in the current period's GDP (treated as investment); after the final product is completed in the next period, only the newly added value is calculated (final product value minus the already included portion) to avoid double counting.

Example: Cotton yarn ($20) is still in inventory at the end of the first period, and the first period's GDP increases by $20. In the second period, the cotton yarn is processed into jeans ($50), and the second period's GDP increases by $30 (= $50 - $20), not $50, otherwise $20 would be double-counted.

Why household new home purchases are included in I and not C:

New homes are durable capital goods that will continue to provide housing services for decades after completion—this is economically similar to firms purchasing machinery and equipment: both are for obtaining a future continuous stream of services, rather than one-time consumption. Therefore, regardless of whether the purchaser is a firm or a household, as long as it is a newly built residence, it is classified as investment.

In addition, putting new home purchases into I, rather than C, makes them easier to track and statistically monitor—the output of the construction industry is already monitored by statistical departments along with business capital expenditures.

  • Buying a new home → Included in I (new production occurs)
  • Buying a used home → Not included in GDP (just asset transfer, no new production)
  • Paying rent → Included in C (purchasing housing services, classified as consumption)

G: Government Spending

Expenditure by all levels of government on goods and services, excluding transfer payments.

Includes: national defense, infrastructure construction, civil servant salaries, government procurement, etc.

Excludes: social security benefits, pensions, unemployment benefits, and other transfer payments—these payments do not correspond to new goods or services being produced. Government spending accounts for about 18% of GDP in the US (2014 data was 18.4%, long-term average about 20%).

X-M: Net Exports

Net Exports = Exports (X) - Imports (M)

Exports (X): Goods and services produced domestically and sold to foreign countries, added to GDP.

Imports (M): Goods and services produced in foreign countries and purchased domestically, subtracted from GDP—because consumption, investment, and government spending may include imported goods, which must be subtracted to ensure only domestic production is counted.

Net exports are positive → Trade Surplus: Exports > Imports Net exports are negative → Trade Deficit: Imports > Exports

Example of import offset:

An American airline spends $100 million to buy an Airbus aircraft (manufactured in Europe):

  • Investment I +$100 million (US firm purchase)
  • Net Exports X-M -$100 million (imports $100 million)
  • Net change in GDP = 0

Logic: The aircraft was not produced in the US and should not be included in US GDP; the offsetting entries ensure this.

1.5 GDP Formula Summary

Expenditures Approach: Y = C + I + G + (X - M)
Income Approach: Y = w + i + r + p
Value-Added Approach: Y = Σ Value Added at Each Stage

The three methods yield the same result—because every market transaction is both an expenditure for the buyer, an income for the seller, and the value added at that stage.

Connection to the Macro Framework

GDP is the most direct indicator for measuring "economic aggregate" in the Dalio framework.

In Dalio's price equation, Price = Total Expenditure ÷ Total Quantity of Goods, and the C+I+G+(X-M) of the GDP expenditure method is precisely the breakdown of this "Total Expenditure." Credit expansion pushes up C and I, central bank policies affect interest rates which in turn affect I, and government fiscal deficits expand G—these are the micro-level correspondences of the debt cycle in the Dalio framework.

GDP itself has two important limitations: ① It is a nominal value, requiring inflation to be removed to obtain real growth (Real GDP vs. Nominal GDP, to be expanded in later chapters); ② It does not measure wealth distribution, environmental degradation, household production, and other non-market activities, and therefore cannot be directly equated with social welfare.

2. Limitations of GDP and Alternative Indicators

2.1 GDP per capita

Before discussing the limitations of GDP, let's introduce a commonly used derivative indicator.

GDP per capita: Divides GDP by the population, used to roughly measure the average standard of living in a country.

GDP per capita = GDP ÷ Population

International migration typically flows from countries with lower GDP per capita to those with higher GDP per capita, indicating that it does capture some differences in living standards. However, it is only a rough indicator—which is the core issue to be discussed in this section.

Nominal GDP per capita also has a limitation in cross-country comparisons: price levels vary widely across different countries, and the same amount of money can buy vastly different things in different countries. Therefore, for cross-country comparisons, it is usually necessary to use GDP per capita PPP (Purchasing Power Parity)—which re-measures countries' output using a unified "international price."

Example: Luxembourg has a very small population, and its nominal GDP is not large, but its GDP per capita far exceeds that of the US; after adjusting for PPP, Qatar ranks higher because local price levels allow the same amount of money to purchase more goods and services.

2.2 Limitations of GDP

GDP is currently the most widely used economic indicator, but it was not designed from the outset to measure social welfare—it measures market output, not Quality of Life. The two are highly correlated but not equivalent.

Economic Activities Not Included

Non-market Transactions: As long as no market exchange occurs, GDP cannot capture it, no matter how high the actual value.

  • Caring for one's own children vs. hiring a nanny: The latter is included in GDP, the former is not, but the services provided are identical.
  • Driving someone oneself vs. taking a taxi: The latter is included in GDP, the former is not.
  • Growing one's own vegetables for consumption: Not included in GDP.

This leads to an interesting statistical phenomenon: as more and more women enter the labor market, services previously provided within the household (cooking, childcare, etc.) shift to the market, and GDP rises accordingly—but this does not mean that the total amount of services actually consumed by society has increased, only that statistical visibility has improved.

Underground Economy and Illegal Activities: Undisclosed cash transactions, black market transactions, etc., are not included in GDP. It is estimated that the US underground economy accounts for about 6.6% of GDP (approximately $2 trillion in 2013).

Dimensions GDP Cannot Measure

  • Income Inequality: GDP per capita is just an average. A 5% GDP growth might mean everyone grew by 5%, or it might mean a few people grew significantly while most saw little change. GDP is completely silent on distribution issues.
  • Leisure Time: US GDP per capita is higher than Germany's, but German workers work hundreds of hours less per year than American workers. Extra vacation time is not reflected in GDP, but it is clearly part of the quality of life.
  • Environmental Quality (Sustainability): GDP includes expenditure on pollution control equipment, but it does not measure whether the air and water are actually cleaner. In a country with high GDP growth but severe environmental degradation, the GDP figures will show prosperity, but the actual quality of life may be declining.
  • Economic Bads: Any negative outcomes of economic activity that create negative value for society, such as air pollution from cars harming human health and the environment; unsustainable economic growth may reduce the quality of life for a country's people.
  • Actual Levels of Health and Education: GDP includes healthcare expenditure, but it does not measure whether life expectancy has increased; GDP includes education expenditure, but it does not measure literacy rates or learning outcomes.
  • Technological Progress and Product Diversity: No matter how rich one was in the 1950s, one couldn't buy a smartphone. GDP cannot measure the improvement in quality of life brought by technological progress, nor can it reflect the welfare gained by consumers from an increase in product variety.

GDP Can Both Overestimate and Underestimate Improvements in Living Standards

Possible Underestimation: Working hours have significantly shortened over the past century; life expectancy and education levels have markedly improved; the value brought by technological progress cannot be fully accounted for.

Possible Overestimation: Non-market services originally provided by households (mainly women) shifted to the market, pushing up GDP, but the actual consumption volume may not have increased; crime, traffic congestion, increasing income inequality, etc., push up some GDP, but reduce the quality of life.

2.3 Related Derivative Indicators

In addition to GDP, there are several other related economic indicators worth understanding:

Gross National Product (GNP): Measures the output of a country's citizens and firms worldwide, regardless of where it is produced.

GNP = GDP + Income of domestic residents from abroad - Income of foreign residents from domestically

GDP focuses on geographical boundaries, while GNP focuses on nationality. For small countries with a large population working abroad and remitting money home, the difference between the two can be significant; for the US, the difference is about 0.2%.

Net National Product (NNP): GNP minus capital depreciation.

NNP = GNP - Depreciation

Depreciation is the value lost by capital as it ages over time. GDP fully includes new capital investment but ignores the wear and tear of old capital, so in economies with faster capital depreciation, GDP may overestimate the actual level of economic activity. NNP, by deducting depreciation, provides an indicator closer to "net new wealth."

National Income: NNP further disaggregated, including all labor and capital income (wages, profits, rent, interest).

⚠️ In practical use, it's not necessary to memorize all definitions, but it's important to note: when comparing data across different countries or different years, you must confirm that the same indicator is being compared (GDP vs. GNP vs. NNP), otherwise the conclusions may be distorted.

2.4 Alternative Composite Indicators

To compensate for the limitations of GDP, various institutions have developed more comprehensive tools for measuring quality of life:

  • Human Development Index (HDI): A composite indicator developed by the UN, covering three dimensions: health (life expectancy), education (years of schooling), and income (GNI per capita).
  • Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI): Builds on GDP by adding the value of positive non-market activities such as volunteer work and household labor, while subtracting negative factors such as crime costs, environmental damage, resource depletion, and income inequality.
  • Happy Planet Index (HPI): Combines well-being surveys, life expectancy, inequality, and ecological footprint to measure how successfully a country is achieving sustainable human well-being.

These indicators often do not align with GDP rankings—countries with high GDP rankings may not necessarily lead in well-being or sustainable development indicators.

2.5 The Role of GDP

GDP measures market output accurately and usefully. It effectively reflects employment status, income levels, and economic expansion or contraction, making it the best single indicator for judging short-term economic conditions.

However, it is not a precise measure of quality of life, and other dimensions need to be supplemented in the following scenarios: comparing countries at different stages of development, assessing the sustainability of growth, analyzing social equity issues, or judging the impact of policies on the actual lives of ordinary people.

A practical cognitive framework: GDP is a thermometer for economic activity, not a barometer for happiness. The two usually move in the same direction, but deviations can occur at the margin.

Connection to the Macro Framework

In the Dalio framework, "economic aggregate" is primarily measured by GDP, which is an appropriate tool when analyzing short-term debt cycles (GDP growth during expansion, GDP contraction during recession). However, when evaluating long-term productivity growth, it is important to note the limitations of GDP—substantial improvements in quality of life due to technological progress, reduced working hours, and environmental improvements are often underestimated or even missing from GDP figures. This is why "real GDP per capita growth" is only a proxy for long-term productivity improvement, rather than directly equivalent to an improvement in living standards.

3. Unemployment Rate

3.1 Population Stratification Structure

To understand the unemployment rate, one must first understand how the population is stratified:

flowchart TD

    B["Eligible Population\nNot institutionalized, not active military"]

    F["Unemployed\nNo work + actively sought work in past 4 weeks"]
    G["Discouraged Workers\nGiven up looking for work"]

    C --> F

    G --> D

    H["Unemployment Rate UR = Unemployed ÷ Labor Force × 100%"]
    I["Labor Force Participation Rate LFPR = Labor Force ÷ Eligible Population × 100%"]
  • Eligible Population: People aged 16 and over, not institutionalized, and not active military personnel.
  • Labor Force: Among the eligible population, those who are working or actively looking for work.
  • Employed: Having worked for pay for at least one hour during the statistical reference week is considered employed.
  • Unemployed: Having no work at all during the reference week, and having actively looked for work in the past four weeks.

⚠️ "Actively looking for work" is the key qualifier. If a person has no job but has not actively sought work in the past four weeks (e.g., has given up looking), they are not counted as unemployed but are classified as "not in the labor force."

3.2 Core Calculation Formulas

Labor Force:

Labor Force = Employed + Unemployed

Unemployment Rate (UR):

Unemployment Rate = Unemployed ÷ Labor Force × 100%

Labor Force Participation Rate (LFPR):

LFPR = Labor Force ÷ Eligible Population × 100%

LFPR measures the proportion of the eligible population actually participating in the labor market and is an important supplementary indicator for understanding the reasons behind changes in the unemployment rate.

Unemployment Rate Measurement (US)

The US Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) surveys approximately 60,000 households each month, asking about their employment status and job search activities, to estimate national data. This is a sample survey, not a census.

3.3 Limitations of the Unemployment Rate

The most important limitation of the unemployment rate is that: its denominator (labor force) itself changes with economic conditions, which can lead to misleading interpretations of the unemployment rate.

Unemployment Rate May Be Underestimated During Economic Downturns

When the economy is in a prolonged slump, some unemployed individuals give up looking for work due to long-term inability to find a job, becoming Discouraged Workers. They exit the labor force statistics, leading to:

  • Unemployed individuals decrease (numerator shrinks)
  • Total labor force also decreases (denominator shrinks)
  • The unemployment rate may actually fall—but this does not mean that employment conditions have improved.

Example: Originally, there were 15 million unemployed individuals and a labor force of 154 million, with an unemployment rate of 9.7%. If 5 million people exit the labor force due to discouragement, the number of unemployed drops to 10 million, the labor force drops to 149 million, and the unemployment rate becomes 6.7%—the economy is actually worse, but the numbers look better.

Unemployment Rate May Be Overestimated During Economic Recoveries

When the economy improves, people who had previously exited the labor force begin looking for work again and are re-counted as unemployed, leading to:

  • Unemployed individuals increase (numerator grows)
  • Total labor force also increases (denominator grows)
  • The unemployment rate may temporarily rise—but this precisely reflects an improving economy and renewed job-seeking confidence.

Therefore, both the unemployment rate and the labor force participation rate should be observed simultaneously to more fully assess the true state of the labor market. If the unemployment rate falls while LFPR also falls, it may indicate an increase in discouraged workers rather than a genuine improvement in employment.

Other Limitations

Underemployed: People who want full-time work but can only find part-time jobs are counted as employed and not included in the unemployment rate, but their resources are not fully utilized.

3.4 Three Types of Unemployment

The unemployment rate can be broken down into two parts: natural rate of unemployment and cyclical unemployment.

Unemployment Rate = Natural Rate of Unemployment (NRU) + Cyclical Unemployment Rate

Natural Rate of Unemployment (NRU)

The unemployment rate that exists even when the economy is operating at its optimal state and full employment. Natural Rate of Unemployment = Frictional Unemployment + Structural Unemployment, and it will never be zero.

Frictional Unemployment

The job search process itself involves friction and time costs—submitting resumes, interviews, waiting for offers—this process is not instantaneous. Even if a worker's skills perfectly match market demand, it still takes time to find a suitable position.

Typical examples: recent college graduates looking for their first job; people who have left one company and are looking for their next job.

Frictional unemployment is not a bad thing—it means workers have the freedom to choose jobs. Technological advancements (such as job search websites, recruitment apps) can reduce frictional unemployment, but will not eliminate it.

Structural Unemployment

There is a structural mismatch between workers' skills and employer demands, usually caused by fundamental changes in the economic structure—new technologies replacing old ones, new industries replacing old ones.

Typical examples: coachmen losing jobs after cars became widespread; coal miners losing jobs after the energy transition; manufacturing workers being replaced by automated machines.

Structural unemployment often takes a longer time to resolve, as workers need to learn new skills or relocate to new areas.

Cyclical Unemployment

Unemployment caused by the business cycle (recession and expansion), equal to the difference between the actual unemployment rate and the natural rate of unemployment:

Cyclical Unemployment Rate = Actual Unemployment Rate - Natural Rate of Unemployment
  • Recession: Cyclical unemployment is positive, actual unemployment rate is higher than the natural rate of unemployment.
  • Expansion: Cyclical unemployment can be negative, actual unemployment rate is lower than the natural rate of unemployment—at this time, firms, in urgent need of personnel, lower hiring standards and employ frictionally unemployed individuals who would normally still be looking for work.

Comparison of Three Types of Unemployment:

TypeCauseElimination Method
FrictionalJob search takes timeImprove information matching efficiency
StructuralSkill-demand mismatchRetraining, industrial transformation
CyclicalEconomic recessionFiscal/monetary policy to stimulate demand

3.5 Full Employment and the Natural Rate of Unemployment

Full Employment does not mean zero unemployment, but rather a state where the economy operates efficiently on the PPC, and the unemployment rate equals the natural rate of unemployment. At this point, only frictional and structural unemployment exist, with no cyclical unemployment.

  • If actual unemployment rate > NRU: The economy is operating inside the PPC, with idle resources.
  • If actual unemployment rate < NRU: The economy is temporarily operating outside the PPC, which is unsustainable.

NRU itself also changes slowly over time—if structural changes accelerate (e.g., technological revolution), NRU may rise; if job matching efficiency improves, NRU may fall.

Connection to the Macro Framework

In the Dalio framework, the unemployment rate is one of the most direct social manifestations of the short-term debt cycle. Credit contraction → corporate income decline → layoffs → cyclical unemployment rises; central bank interest rate cuts or government fiscal stimulus → demand recovery → cyclical unemployment falls. Frictional and structural unemployment correspond to the structural constraints of "productivity growth" in the Dalio framework—technological substitution and industrial transformation continuously generate structural unemployment, which is an unavoidable byproduct of economic progress and cannot be eliminated through monetary or fiscal policy, only mitigated through education and retraining.

4. Inflation

Inflation: A sustained increase in the general price level in an economy, leading to a decrease in the purchasing power of money.

Note two qualifiers: general (not just one commodity's price increase) and sustained (not short-term fluctuations).

Inflation Rate: The percentage change in the price level from one period to the next.

Inflation Rate = (New Price Level - Old Price Level) ÷ Old Price Level × 100%

⚠️ Historically, the term "inflation" originally referred to the expansion of the money supply (monetary type), but in modern economics, inflation defaults to price inflation. The two are related but not entirely equivalent—money supply growth does not necessarily lead to immediate price increases, and in the short term, it can also be affected by supply shocks and other factors.

4.1 CPI: Measuring Inflation

The Logic of the Market Basket

Measuring inflation requires a benchmark—one cannot just look at the price of a single commodity, but rather the price changes of a whole set of goods and services purchased by a representative consumer.

Consumer Price Index (CPI): Tracks the total cost of a fixed "basket" of goods and services over time, measuring changes in the cost of living for a typical urban household.

Market Basket: A fixed combination of goods used to calculate CPI, which remains constant each year. The weight of each type of good in the basket is based on the actual expenditure proportion of consumers—goods on which consumers spend more money have a greater impact on CPI.

Base Year: The year used as a reference for comparison, whose CPI is set to 100. The choice of which year to use as the base year is arbitrary and does not affect the calculation of the inflation rate.

Steps to Calculate CPI

  1. Determine the composition and quantity of goods in the basket.
  2. Calculate the total cost of purchasing the basket in the base year.
  3. Calculate the total cost of purchasing the basket in the target year.
  4. CPI = (Cost in Target Year ÷ Cost in Base Year) × 100

Example (Simplified):

Assume the basket contains only two goods:

GoodQuantityBase Year Unit PriceTarget Year Unit Price
Bread10 loaves$2$3
Milk5 liters$4$4
Base Year Cost = 10×$2 + 5×$4 = $40
Target Year Cost = 10×$3 + 5×$4 = $50

CPI (Target Year) = ($50 ÷ $40) × 100 = 125

A CPI of 125 means: compared to the base year, purchasing the same basket now costs 25% more.

Calculating the Inflation Rate

With CPI for two periods, the inflation rate calculation is a standard percentage change:

Inflation Rate = (CPI₂ - CPI₁) ÷ CPI₁ × 100%

Example: If CPI in one year is 125, and the next year it is 130:

Inflation Rate = (130 - 125) ÷ 125 × 100% = 4%

⚠️ You cannot directly subtract CPI numbers to get the inflation rate (e.g., 130-125=5%); you must divide by the base period CPI. The error is small when the numbers are close, but for precise calculation, the formula must be used.

⚠️ Compound inflation calculation: If inflation is 2% annually, prices will not rise by 20% after 10 years, but by approximately 22% (compound effect).

4.2 Nominal Variables and Real Variables

This is the core tool for understanding the impact of inflation, and also key to connecting inflation with investment decisions.

Nominal Variables: Book figures not adjusted for inflation, such as nominal wages, nominal interest rates.

Real Variables: Variables adjusted for inflation, reflecting true purchasing power.

Real Interest Rate ≈ Nominal Interest Rate - Inflation Rate
Real Wage Growth ≈ Nominal Wage Growth - Inflation Rate

Example: Bank deposit nominal interest rate 3%, inflation rate 5%, real interest rate = 3% - 5% = -2%. On paper, you're earning money, but your actual purchasing power is shrinking.

Purchasing Power: The amount of goods that a certain amount of money can actually buy. The higher the inflation rate, the faster the purchasing power of money declines.

4.3 Limitations of CPI

CPI is currently the most widely used tool for measuring inflation, but it has two systematic biases that cause it to tend to overestimate the true rise in the cost of living.

Substitution Bias

CPI assumes that consumers purchase the exact same basket of goods each year, but in reality, when the price of a certain good rises, consumers will switch to relatively cheaper substitutes. A fixed basket cannot capture this behavioral adjustment, thus overestimating the increase in the cost of living.

Example: Beef prices rise, consumers switch to chicken. CPI still calculates based on the original beef purchase quantity, but actual consumers have already reduced their beef expenditure.

Quality/New Goods Bias

A fixed basket cannot fully reflect improvements in product quality and the emergence of new goods.

Quality Improvement: Today's cars have more safety features than cars 20 years ago, are more expensive but also more functional. CPI may count the entire price increase as inflation, ignoring the portion due to quality improvement.

New Goods: The update of basket goods lags behind the market. New products are most expensive when first launched, then gradually decrease in price. However, the CPI basket update lags, and by the time new products are included, prices have already fallen for some time, causing CPI to miss this period of price decline and thus overestimate inflation.

Combining these two biases, CPI may overestimate true inflation by about 0.5 percentage points annually.

4.4 Choosing Different Price Indices Based on Purpose

This section introduces various price indices, with the core logic being: there is no "best" index, only the index "most suitable for the problem."

IndexEnglishMeasured ObjectMain Use
Consumer Price IndexCPIBasket of goods purchased by urban householdsMeasures household cost of living, "inflation rate" in news reports
Core Inflation IndexCore CPICPI excluding food and energyCentral bank policy decisions, filters short-term fluctuations
GDP DeflatorGDP DeflatorPrices of all GDP componentsMost comprehensive measure of price level, used to calculate real GDP
Producer Price IndexPPIRaw materials and intermediate goods purchased by producersLeading indicator for CPI, predicts consumer inflation trends
Employment Cost IndexECIWages in the labor marketMeasures labor cost inflation

Why Core Inflation is Needed:

Food and energy prices fluctuate sharply due to short-term factors such as weather and geopolitics. If a hurricane pushes up oil prices for a month, this does not mean the underlying inflation trend has changed, and the central bank should not adjust interest rate policy based on it. Core inflation filters out this noise, allowing policymakers to see more persistent inflation trends.

Why the GDP Deflator is Not Suitable for Measuring Cost of Living:

It includes goods that households do not purchase (such as military aircraft, factory equipment), and therefore cannot accurately reflect changes in the cost of living for ordinary consumers. However, it is the most accurate tool for calculating Real GDP (GDP after removing inflation).

The Logic of PPI Leading CPI:

Rising production costs (PPI increase) usually take several months to transmit to consumer prices (CPI increase). Observing PPI trends can help predict the direction of CPI changes in advance.

4.5 Winners and Losers of Inflation

Anticipated vs. Unanticipated Inflation

The impact of inflation on different groups depends on a key premise: whether inflation is anticipated.

Anticipated Inflation: When borrowers and lenders sign contracts, inflation expectations have already been incorporated into the nominal interest rate, so the actual impact is limited. If both parties anticipate 5% inflation, the nominal interest rate will include this 5% compensation, the real interest rate will remain largely unchanged, and wealth will not be unexpectedly redistributed.

Unanticipated Inflation: Actual inflation deviates from expectations, leading to an unexpected redistribution of wealth between borrowers and lenders. This is the fundamental reason why inflation truly creates "winners and losers."

Impact Mechanism on Borrowers and Lenders

Take fixed-rate lending as an example: Today, a lender lends $100, agreeing to receive $110 back in one year (nominal interest rate 10%). At this time, $100 happens to equal the price of a basket of goods.

Inflation ScenarioBasket Price After One YearHow Many Baskets Can $110 BuyLender's Real Return
No inflation$1001.1 baskets+10%
Moderate inflation (2%)$102Approx. 1.08 basketsApprox. +8%
Extreme inflation (120%)$2200.5 baskets-50%
Extreme deflation (45%)$552 baskets+100%

Pattern:

  • Unanticipated inflation → Borrowers benefit, lenders lose: Borrowers repay debt with money of lower purchasing power, reducing their real debt burden; lenders receive money with lower purchasing power than when it was lent out.
  • Unanticipated deflation/disinflation → Lenders benefit, borrowers lose: Lenders receive money with higher purchasing power than anticipated; borrowers must repay debt with money of higher purchasing power, increasing their real interest burden.

Other Affected Groups

  • Losers:
    • Those holding large amounts of cash: The purchasing power of cash directly shrinks with inflation.
    • Those with fixed nominal incomes (e.g., fixed pensions): Their real purchasing power is continuously eroded; 1-2% inflation may seem mild, but its cumulative effect over decades is substantial.
  • Winners:
    • Those holding real assets (real estate, gold): Asset prices typically rise with inflation, preserving real wealth relatively.

Implications for Investment: When inflation rises and nominal interest rates remain unchanged, real interest rates fall. The opportunity cost of holding gold, which does not generate cash flow, decreases, and its attractiveness increases—this is precisely the mechanism behind the inverse relationship between gold and real interest rates.

4.6 Harms of Inflation and Extreme Scenarios

Harms of Inflation

  • Blurred Price Signals
    Prices are signals that convey supply and demand information in a market economy. During high inflation, it becomes difficult to distinguish how much of a price change is due to inflation and how much is due to real supply and demand shifts. Businesses and consumers cannot accurately read price signals, resource allocation efficiency decreases, and the speed at which the market adjusts to equilibrium also slows down.
  • Difficulty in Long-Term Planning
    Inflation increases future uncertainty. Retirement planning, corporate investment decisions, and long-term lending contracts all require predictions of future price levels. The higher and more unstable inflation is, the greater the difficulty and risk of long-term planning. Businesses may shift their focus to hedging inflation risk rather than improving production efficiency.

Disinflation

The inflation rate is falling but still positive; prices are still rising, just at a slower pace. When policymakers say "lower inflation," the goal is disinflation, not deflation.

Deflation

The absolute decrease in the price level, meaning the inflation rate is negative.

On the surface, falling prices due to deflation seem beneficial to consumers, but it often comes with serious economic problems:

Consumers expect "tomorrow will be cheaper" → postpone purchases → corporate revenue falls → layoffs → fewer people consume → prices continue to fall → reinforcing spiral

Deflation also renders monetary policy ineffective: nominal interest rates cannot be significantly negative, and deflation means real interest rates automatically rise, suppressing borrowing and investment. This is why most policymakers prioritize avoiding deflation over controlling inflation.

Hyperinflation

Hyperinflation is extremely high inflation, usually associated with excessive government money printing, often occurring during transitions from planned to market economies or when government finances are completely out of control. Under hyperinflation, money virtually loses its value, price signals completely collapse, economic activity is paralyzed, and social stability is severely threatened.

Germany in 1923 and Zimbabwe in the 2000s are textbook examples.

Stagflation

Stagflation is an abnormal state where inflation and economic stagnation occur simultaneously—prices rise while economic growth slows and unemployment increases.

The classic trigger mechanism is a supply-side shock (e.g., oil embargo):

Supply sharp decrease → Price increase (inflation) → Production costs rise → Corporate profits fall → Layoffs → Demand falls → Economic stagnation

Stagflation is the most difficult situation for central banks to deal with: raising interest rates can suppress inflation but will further depress an already weak economy; cutting interest rates can stimulate the economy but will exacerbate inflation. The two goals conflict, and there is no win-win solution for monetary policy.

The US oil crisis of the 1970s is the most typical historical example.

Connection to the Macro Framework

In the Dalio framework, inflation is a direct product of credit expansion—more credit creation means more total expenditure, pushing up price levels. CPI is the most direct indicator for measuring this process.

The real interest rate (nominal interest rate - inflation rate) is a core tool for understanding the prices of assets such as gold, bonds, and exchange rates—this is also the practical significance of "nominal vs. real variables" in this section. Continuously monitoring inflation data (CPI, core inflation, PPI) and understanding the meaning of each indicator is a fundamental ability for judging central bank policy direction and, consequently, asset allocation timing.

5. Nominal GDP and Real GDP

5.1 Why Distinguish Between Nominal and Real

GDP measures output in monetary terms, but the purchasing power of money changes with inflation. If this year's GDP grows from $1,000 to $1,200, a 20% increase, this could be because output genuinely increased, or merely because prices rose, or both.

Take a simplified economy that only produces apples as an example:

Year OneYear Two
GDP$1,000$1,200
Apple Price$0.50/lb$0.55/lb
Apple Output2,000 lbs2,182 lbs

Nominal GDP grew by 20%, but actual output only grew by about 9%. If nominal and real are not distinguished, the true economic growth will be overestimated.

Relationship Between Nominal GDP and Real GDP

In the base year, Nominal GDP = Real GDP (Deflator = 100, the two are identical).

After the base year, if there is inflation, nominal GDP will increase with rising prices, and the gap with real GDP will gradually widen. The slope of the nominal GDP curve appears steeper, but a significant portion of this "growth" is merely an illusion of price increases.

⚠️ When seeing GDP growth figures, it is crucial to first confirm whether it is nominal or real. Nominal GDP growth does not necessarily mean a genuine increase in output—it might just be inflation pushing up prices. Only real GDP growth represents the expansion of true output.

5.2 Definitions

Nominal GDP

GDP calculated using current year's actual prices, also known as "current price GDP." It reflects the total market transactions for the year but includes the impact of price changes, so it cannot solely reflect changes in output quantity.

Real GDP

GDP calculated using prices from a specific base year, also known as "constant price GDP." It removes the impact of price changes, truly reflecting changes in output quantity, and is an indicator of real economic growth.

GDP Deflator

A price index that tracks the average price level of all final goods and services within a country. The deflator for the base year is defined as 100, and the deflator for other years reflects the degree of price change relative to the base year.

The GDP deflator is derived from the ratio of nominal GDP to real GDP:

GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP ÷ Real GDP) × 100
  • Deflator > 100: Prices are higher than the base year, indicating inflation.
  • Deflator < 100: Prices are lower than the base year, indicating deflation.

5.3 Method for Calculating Real GDP: Base-Year Price Weighting Method

Multiply the output of each good in the target year by the price in the base year, and sum them up to get real GDP. This method eliminates price changes, reflecting only the true change in output quantity.

Calculation Steps:

  1. Determine the base year and record the prices of each good in the base year.
  2. Record the output of each good in the target year.
  3. Multiply base year price × target year output, then sum.

Continuing with the apple example above: Year Two output is 2,182 lbs, calculated using Year One price of $0.50:

Real GDP (Year Two, using Year One prices) = 2,182 × $0.50 = $1,091

Compared to nominal GDP ($1,200), the real growth is only $91, or about 9%, not the apparent 20%.

Limitation: This method may overestimate real GDP growth. If the price of a certain good continuously decreases over time (e.g., electronic products), using a high price from long ago as the base will exaggerate that good's contribution to GDP, making real GDP growth appear higher than it actually is.

Example:

Assume the price of apples falls from $0.50 in Year One to $0.25 in Year Three, but output increases from 2,000 lbs to 2,200 lbs.

Calculated using Year Three's current price: 2,200 × $0.25 = $550 Calculated using Year One's base price: 2,200 × $0.50 = $1,100

Although the output increase is the same 200 lbs, the value calculated using the base year price is $1,100, which is double the $550 calculated using the current year price. Real GDP growth is significantly overestimated.

⚠️ Note: What is overestimated here is the monetary value of output, not the output quantity itself. Output quantity is objective, but using different prices to measure its value will yield different results. In practice, statistical agencies reduce this bias by regularly updating the base year or using more complex chain-weighted methods to reduce this bias.

Connection to the Macro Framework

The Dalio framework repeatedly emphasizes the distinction between nominal and real values—wages may be rising nominally, but real purchasing power might be shrinking; debt is accounted for at nominal value, but the real repayment pressure is determined by real value. The distinction between nominal GDP and real GDP is a concrete manifestation of this principle at the macroeconomic statistical level. Real GDP growth is a core indicator for measuring a country's true increase in productivity.

6. Business Cycle

Plotting the change in real GDP over time reveals two superimposed trends:

Long-term trend line: Driven by population growth and productivity improvements, real GDP steadily rises in the long run.

Short-term fluctuations: Real GDP fluctuates above and below the long-term trend line, sometimes higher, sometimes lower.

These short-term fluctuations around the trend line are called the Business Cycle.

The word "cycle" can be somewhat misleading—it is not a regular, predictable pattern like a sine wave. The amplitude and duration of business cycles are highly irregular and difficult to predict. The time interval between adjacent peaks or troughs is not fixed, which is the fundamental reason why business cycles are hard to forecast.

6.1 Four Phases and Two Turning Points of the Business Cycle

Phase/Turning PointDefinition
ExpansionReal GDP increases, unemployment rate decreases
PeakTurning point where expansion ends and contraction begins
RecessionReal GDP decreases, unemployment rate increases
TroughTurning point where recession ends and recovery begins
RecoveryStarts from the trough, GDP rises again until it returns to potential output level

Recession and Depression: A recession refers to a significant decline in real GDP; if the recession is extremely deep and prolonged, it is called a depression. The Great Depression of the 1930s is the most typical example.

In the United States, the official dating of business cycles is handled by the private research institution National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER). It is worth noting that the actual effects of a severe recession often persist for a considerable time after the official end date announced by the NBER.

6.2 Potential Output and Output Gap

Potential Output: The level of real GDP an economy can achieve when all its resources are fully and efficiently utilized, also known as "full employment output." At this point, the unemployment rate precisely equals the natural rate of unemployment (NRU), and there is no cyclical unemployment. In a business cycle chart, potential output corresponds to the long-term growth trend line.

Output Gap: The difference between actual output and potential output.

  • Positive Output Gap: Actual output > Potential output, the business cycle curve is above the trend line. At this point, the economy is "overheating"—the unemployment rate is below NRU, businesses are operating beyond capacity, and resources are overutilized. This state is unsustainable and will eventually fall back from a peak into a recession.
  • Negative Output Gap: Actual output < Potential output, the business cycle curve is below the trend line. At this point, the unemployment rate is above NRU, the economy is operating inside the PPC, and there are idle resources.

6.3 Correspondence with PPC

Each stage of the business cycle can be visually represented using the PPC:

Business Cycle PositionCorresponding PPC PositionMeaning
Actual Output = Potential OutputPoint on the PPCFull employment, efficient resource utilization
Positive Output Gap (near peak)Point outside the PPCOverutilization of resources, unsustainable
Negative Output Gap (recession)Point inside the PPCIdle resources, cyclical unemployment exists
RecoveryMoves from inside the PPC to a point on the PPCOutput recovers, unemployment rate falls

6.4 Expansion ≠ Economic Growth

This is one of the most common misconceptions.

Expansion is the process of recovering from a recessionary trough, an increase in real output during short-term fluctuations, corresponding to the upward segment of the business cycle curve. Even if the economy is in an expansion phase, if actual output has not yet returned to the potential output level, a negative output gap still exists.

Economic Growth is the increase in potential output itself—the upward shift of the trend line, or the outward shift of the entire PPC. This comes from long-term structural factors such as population growth, technological progress, and capital accumulation, and is unrelated to short-term cyclical fluctuations.

Example: If a country declines from a potential output level of $2 trillion to $1.9 trillion, then recovers back to $2 trillion, this is merely a recovery from short-term fluctuations, not economic growth. If potential output itself increases from $2 trillion to $2.5 trillion, that is true economic growth.

6.5 Human Emotion and the Business Cycle

Traditional economic models exclude human emotion, but emotion can be one of the most important factors driving the business cycle.

Emotional Evolution During Expansion:

At the end of a recession, people are still wary, skeptical of recovery, and hesitant to consume and invest. As time passes, painful memories fade, people begin to rebuild confidence, consumption and investment increase, and the economy expands further. In the later stages of expansion, long after the last recession, people start to underestimate risks, become overly optimistic, and tend to extrapolate recent growth into the future.

Emotional Evolution During Peak and Recession:

Optimism → Excitement → Euphoria → Exhilaration → Anxiety → Denial → Fear → Panic → Despair

Emotional Evolution During Trough and Recovery:

Despair → Depression → Hope → Relief → Optimism (new cycle)

This emotional cycle highly aligns with stock market cycles and is closely linked to real economic cycles. From an investment perspective, the "best time to invest" often appears near the trough when emotions are most pessimistic, while the "most dangerous time" often appears near the peak when emotions are most euphoric.

⚠️ Traditional economic models of aggregate supply and aggregate demand do not include emotional factors, which is one of their significant limitations. Behavioral Economics is an emerging field dedicated to studying the impact of emotions and irrational behavior on the economy.

Connection to the Macro Framework

The business cycle is the statistical manifestation of the short-term debt cycle (5-8 years) in the Dalio framework. Credit expansion pushes output above the trend line (positive output gap), while credit contraction leads to output below the trend line (negative output gap). The emotional analysis in this section aligns closely with the Dalio framework—Dalio also emphasizes that market participants' emotions and expectations are core forces driving cycles, rather than purely economic variables. The long-term upward trend of potential output, in turn, corresponds to the underlying trend line of "productivity growth" in the Dalio framework.
