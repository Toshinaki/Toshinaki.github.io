1. What is Economics

The core question economics studies: how individuals and societies make choices under conditions of limited resources.

The keyword in this definition is scarcity: human wants are unlimited, but the resources needed to satisfy them are limited. It is this contradiction that makes "how to allocate" a question that must be answered.

Any economic system must answer three basic questions:

  • What to produce with limited resources?
  • How to produce?
  • How to distribute the output?

1.1 Scarcity

A scarce resource is one for which, if there were no cost constraints, people's demand would exceed its actual supply. The opposite is a free resource—one that is, in some sense, almost infinitely abundant, and one person's use does not affect others'.

The object of study in economics is scarce resources, including: food, water, labor, land, time, capital.

The boundary of free resources is relative—atmospheric oxygen on Earth is a free resource for ordinary people, but a scarce resource for residents of a lunar base. Scarcity depends on the specific context, not an inherent attribute of the resource.

1.2 Microeconomics vs Macroeconomics

MicroeconomicsMacroeconomics
Object of StudyIndividual agents (individuals, households, firms)Overall economy
Core QuestionsHow scarce resources are allocated, how prices and markets are affectedOverall economic performance, such as growth, inflation, unemployment, interest rates
PerspectiveBottom-upTop-down
Typical QuestionsImpact of a change in a specific good's priceWhether taxes should be raised, how monetary policy should be set

Adam Smith's "Invisible Hand" involves two levels: at the micro level, individuals pursue their self-interest; at the macro level, these self-interested actions may collectively promote social welfare—even if this is not the individuals' subjective intention.

1.3 Positive Statement vs Normative Statement

This is a metacognitive tool for economic analysis, permeating all discussions.

Positive Statement: A statement that can be tested, describing "what is," independent of personal values. It may be true or false, but can be verified through data and experiments.

Example: "Welfare programs reduce people's work incentives"—this can be tested by comparing data from groups with and without welfare programs, regardless of whether the conclusion is true or false.

Normative Statement: A statement based on value judgments, describing "what ought to be," an opinion or moral stance that cannot be objectively tested.

Example: "Giving welfare to people unwilling to work is wrong and unfair"—this involves value judgments and cannot be tested for right or wrong with data.

⚠️ Identification Tip: Statements containing "should," "fair/unfair," or "right/wrong" are usually normative statements; other statements, as long as they describe observable cause-and-effect relationships, are usually positive statements—even if the speaker has a clear value bias.

Example: "Taxing the rich will promote economic growth" and "Taxing the rich will inhibit economic growth" are opposite in stance, but both are positive statements—because both are testable causal claims.

Disagreements among economists usually stem from: ① different empirical judgments about positive statements, or ② different normative stances. Distinguishing these two types of disagreements helps understand the true nature of the debate.

1.4 Economic Models

What is a Model

Economics borrows from other sciences—using simplified models to understand complex systems.

Chemistry has the ideal gas law, simplifying the interactions of trillions of particles into equations with a few variables; biology uses fruit flies and mice to study problems that cannot be directly experimented on humans; economics also uses models—establishing an analytical framework in complex reality through simplifying assumptions and mathematization.

Common simplifying assumptions in economic models include:

  • All agents are rational
  • Everyone has the same or complete information
  • All other things being equal (Ceteris Paribus, meaning "other conditions being the same")

Value of Models

  • Clarifies thinking, allowing for mathematical expression and derivation
  • Can identify relationships between key variables
  • Can make predictions for policy discussions

Limitations of Models

This is a more important understanding than the models themselves:

1. Models are based on assumptions, and assumptions can be wrong

"Everyone is rational" does not hold true in reality. The entire field of Behavioral Economics is built on studying human "irrational" choices.

2. Macroeconomic models are particularly dangerous

The micro level already simplifies complex human behavior once; the macro level aggregates the behavior of millions of people on top of that—uncertainty is amplified exponentially.

3. Economics cannot conduct experiments like physics

You cannot randomly divide a thousand similar economies into two groups, apply a policy to one, and then observe and compare the results. This makes determining causality inherently more difficult than in physics or chemistry.

⚠️ Mathematics may appear rigorous, but it does not guarantee correct conclusions. If the assumptions themselves are flawed, even precise mathematics merely builds a precise edifice of error on a faulty foundation. Always ask: What are the core assumptions of this model? Are they valid in the current context?

Two maxims worth remembering:

"An economist is an expert who states the obvious in terms of the incomprehensible."—Alfred Knopf

"An economist is an expert who will know tomorrow why the things he predicted yesterday didn't happen today."—Lawrence J. Peter

2. Production Possibilities Curve and Opportunity Cost

2.1 Production Possibilities Curve

The Production Possibilities Curve (PPC) is a graphical model that illustrates all possible combinations of two goods an economy (individual, firm, or country) can produce, given fixed resources and technology.

Its core function is to visualize scarcity and opportunity cost—each point on the curve represents a production combination of two goods, assuming all resources are fully utilized.

Example (Hunter-Gatherer's Rabbits and Berries):

Suppose a hunter-gatherer allocates time each day between hunting rabbits and gathering berries. Their possible output combinations are as follows:

ScenarioRabbits (units)Berries (units)
A50
B4100
C3180
D2240
E1280
F0300

Connecting these points yields the PPC. All analysis is conducted under the assumption of Ceteris Paribus—meaning total time, technology, tools, and all other variables remain constant.

Meaning of Regions on the Curve

  • Points on the PPC: Efficient—resources are fully utilized, and it's impossible to increase the production of one good without decreasing the production of another.
  • Points inside the PPC: Inefficient—resources are idle or wasted, and output can be increased without sacrificing any other good. This corresponds to a recession or underutilization of resources in the economy.
  • Points outside the PPC: Impossible/Unattainable—cannot be achieved with current resources and technology.

⚠️ Common misconception: Moving from an inefficient point to a point on the curve (from inefficient to efficient) is not economic growth; it merely means better utilization of existing resources. Economic growth refers to the entire curve shifting outward.

Curve Shape and Types of Opportunity Cost

The shape of the PPC reveals the pattern of opportunity cost:

1. Constant Opportunity Cost PPC is a straight line. For each additional unit of one good produced, the quantity of the other good sacrificed remains constant. Example: For each rabbit caught, 60 berries are always sacrificed.

2. Increasing Opportunity Cost PPC is bowed outward (convex to the origin). As the production of one good increases, the quantity of the other good that must be sacrificed for each additional unit increases. This is the most common shape.

3. Decreasing Opportunity Cost PPC is bowed inward (concave to the origin). As the production of one good increases, the quantity of the other good that must be sacrificed for each additional unit decreases. Reason: Economies of scale, learning effects—becoming more proficient, marginal cost decreases.

2.2 Opportunity Cost

Opportunity Cost: The value of the best alternative sacrificed when making a choice.

Cost in economics is not just monetary cost, but "what you had to give up".

Example: In scenario E (1 rabbit, 280 berries), wanting to catch 1 more rabbit (moving to scenario D):

  • Rabbits +1 (from 1 to 2)
  • Berries -40 (from 280 to 240)
  • Therefore: Opportunity cost of catching 1 more rabbit = 40 berries

Marginal Cost

Marginal Cost: The opportunity cost of producing 1 additional unit of a good.

机会成本（每单位X）= ΔY ÷ ΔX
                  = (Y₁ - Y₂) ÷ (X₂ - X₁)

Example: From scenario E (1 rabbit, 280 berries) to scenario D (2 rabbits, 240 berries):

机会成本 = (280 - 240) ÷ (2 - 1)
         = 40 ÷ 1
         = 40 颗浆果 / 只兔子

Intuitive Explanation of Increasing Opportunity Cost

In the example of rabbits and berries above, from F to E to D... for each additional rabbit caught, more berries are sacrificed:

MovementAdditional RabbitsBerries SacrificedOpportunity Cost
F → E12020 berries/rabbit
E → D14040 berries/rabbit
D → C16060 berries/rabbit
C → B18080 berries/rabbit
B → A1100100 berries/rabbit

Opportunity cost is increasing. The reason lies in the specificity of resources: initially, the berries sacrificed were the hardest to gather, and the rabbits caught were the easiest; later, the berries sacrificed were increasingly easier to gather, while the rabbits to be caught were increasingly harder.

2.3 PPC as a National Economic Model

Extending the PPC to the national level: a country uses its limited resources (land, labor, capital, technology) to make choices between different goods.

Economic Growth

The entire PPC curve shifts outward, representing an increase in the economy's ability to produce more goods.

Triggering factors:

  • Increase in land (natural resources)
  • Increase in capital (more factories, machinery)
  • Increase in labor (population growth, immigration)
  • Technological advancement (increase in Productivity/Technology)
  • Improvement in entrepreneurship (better ways of combining resources)

Economic Contraction

The PPC curve shifts inward, representing a decrease in productive capacity.

Triggering factors: war (destruction of capital), resource depletion, population decline, technological regression.

Recession

The economy moves from a point on the curve to a point inside the curve, representing underutilization of existing resources. The curve itself does not move; rather, the economy's operating point deviates from the efficiency frontier.

2.4 Connection to Macroeconomic Frameworks

The PPC is the most intuitive model for understanding scarcity and trade-offs, transforming the abstract concept of "limited resources" into a quantifiable and visualizable tool.

In subsequent analyses, the PPC way of thinking will appear in different forms:

  • The "guns vs. butter" trade-off (military spending vs. civilian spending) in fiscal policy discussions is a typical PPC trade-off.
  • A country's long-term economic growth path is essentially a process of the PPC continuously shifting outward—this perfectly corresponds to Dalio's framework where "productivity growth is the sole source of long-term wealth".
  • Recession (operating inside the PPC) corresponds to the credit contraction and idle resource phases in Dalio's framework.

3. Comparative Advantage and Gains from Trade

3.1 Comparative Advantage is the True Basis for Specialization

Suppose Country A and Country B can both produce pants and shirts. The maximum output under the same input conditions is as follows:

Pants (units/day)Shirts (units/day)
Country A2010
Country B3045

Intuitively, one might think that Country B, having higher output in both goods, should produce everything. This intuition corresponds to Absolute Advantage—producing more with the same inputs, and Country B indeed has an absolute advantage in both pants and shirts.

However, absolute advantage is not the basis for specialization. What truly matters is Comparative Advantage—who can produce a certain good at a lower opportunity cost.

Calculate the opportunity cost for both countries:

生产1单位 X 的机会成本 = Y 的最大产量 ÷ X 的最大产量
Opportunity cost of producing 1 pair of pantsOpportunity cost of producing 1 shirt
Country A10 ÷ 20 = 1/2 shirt20 ÷ 10 = 2 pairs of pants
Country B45 ÷ 30 = 3/2 shirts30 ÷ 45 = 2/3 pairs of pants

Comparing the two countries: Country A only needs to give up 1/2 shirt to produce 1 pair of pants, while Country B needs to give up 3/2 shirts—Country A has a lower opportunity cost for pants, so Country A has a comparative advantage in pants. Conversely, Country B only needs to give up 2/3 pairs of pants to produce 1 shirt, while Country A needs to give up 2 pairs—Country B has a comparative advantage in shirts.

This conclusion is graphically represented by the slope of the PPC: Country A's slope (absolute value) is smaller, indicating fewer shirts are sacrificed for each additional pair of pants produced. The difference in the slopes of the two lines is the intuitive source of comparative advantage.

3.2 Specialization, Terms of Trade, and Gains from Trade

Once comparative advantage is clear, the specialization plan is determined: Country A specializes in producing pants, and Country B specializes in producing shirts.

  • Country A fully produces pants: 20 pairs/day, 0 shirts
  • Country B fully produces shirts: 0 pairs of pants, 45 units/day

Both countries need each other's goods, leading to trade. The key question is: what terms of trade are beneficial for both parties?

The answer depends on their respective opportunity costs. For Country A, producing 1 shirt itself means giving up 2 pairs of pants; as long as the trade price is less than 2 pairs of pants, buying shirts from Country B is more cost-effective than producing them itself. For Country B, producing 1 pair of pants itself means giving up 3/2 shirts; as long as the trade price is less than 3/2 shirts, buying pants from Country A is more cost-effective than producing them itself.

Therefore, the reasonable terms of trade range is:

A国机会成本 < 裤子价格 < B国机会成本
1/2件衬衫 < 裤子价格 < 3/2件衬衫

Taking a midpoint, both parties agree to trade at 1 pair of pants for 1 shirt.

Suppose Country A trades 15 pairs of pants for 15 shirts. The final consumption combinations for each are:

PantsShirts
Country A (after trade)515
Country B (after trade)1530

If Country A did not trade, when producing 5 pairs of pants, it could at most produce 7.5 shirts simultaneously (all remaining resources used for shirts); but after trade, it obtained 15 shirts. The same applies to Country B. Both countries reached consumption points beyond their respective PPCs—this is the essence of gains from trade: specialization increases global total output, and trade allows both parties to consume combinations that would be unattainable if they produced alone.

3.3 Boundary Cases

The premise for comparative advantage is that the opportunity costs of the two countries are different. If opportunity costs are identical, there is no comparative advantage, and trade yields no additional benefits.

Example:

Apples (units/day)Bananas (units/day)
Country A36
Country B24

Calculate opportunity cost:

Opportunity cost of producing 1 appleOpportunity cost of producing 1 banana
Country A6 ÷ 3 = 2 bananas3 ÷ 6 = 1/2 apple
Country B4 ÷ 2 = 2 bananas2 ÷ 4 = 1/2 apple

The opportunity costs of both countries are identical, so there is no comparative advantage. Graphically, the slopes of the two PPCs are the same and parallel to each other—regardless of the trade price, neither party can consume beyond their respective PPCs.

⚠️ Note: Country B produces less of both goods than Country A (absolute disadvantage), but this is irrelevant to whether there is a comparative advantage. Comparative advantage only considers opportunity cost, not absolute output.

Calculation from Input Table

Sometimes the given conditions are not output, but rather the labor hours required to produce each unit of a good (input table). The calculation logic remains the same; simply convert to an output table first.

Example:

Labor hours required to produce 1 toy carLabor hours required to produce 1 belt
Country A2 hours1 hour
Country B4 hours3 hours

Assuming 8 working hours per day, convert to an output table:

每天产量 = 每日工时 ÷ 每件所需工时
Toy cars (units/day)Belts (units/day)
Country A8 ÷ 2 = 48 ÷ 1 = 8
Country B8 ÷ 4 = 28 ÷ 3 = 2.67

Then calculate opportunity cost:

Opportunity cost of producing 1 toy carOpportunity cost of producing 1 belt
Country A8 ÷ 4 = 2 belts4 ÷ 8 = 1/2 toy car
Country B2.67 ÷ 2 = 4/3 belts2 ÷ 2.67 = 3/4 toy car

Country B's opportunity cost of producing toy cars (4/3 belts) is lower than Country A's (2 belts) → Country B has a comparative advantage in toy cars.

Country A's opportunity cost of producing belts (1/2 toy car) is lower than Country B's (3/4 toy car) → Country A has a comparative advantage in belts.

It is worth noting that Country A has an absolute advantage in both goods (4 cars > 2 cars, 8 belts > 2.67 belts), but Country B still has a comparative advantage in toy cars. This again illustrates that absolute advantage and comparative advantage are two different things, and the basis for specialization is always opportunity cost, not the level of output.

Connection to Macroeconomic Frameworks

The theory of comparative advantage explains why international specialization and trade can increase global total output—this directly corresponds to Dalio's framework where "productivity growth is the sole source of long-term wealth": specialization itself is a form of productivity enhancement. When the trade system is impacted (tariff wars, shipping blockades), countries are forced to abandon comparative advantage and turn to inefficient self-sufficiency, leading to a decline in global productivity. This is the underlying mechanism by which trade friction drags down economic growth.

4. Demand

4.1 Demand and Quantity Demanded

When studying demand theory, the easiest mistake to make is confusing two concepts:

  • Demand is the entire curve, describing all quantities buyers are willing and able to purchase at all possible prices—that is, the complete relationship between price and quantity demanded.
  • Quantity Demanded is a specific point on the curve, corresponding to the particular quantity buyers are willing to purchase at a given price.

Their mechanisms of change are completely different:

  • Change in Quantity Demanded: Caused by a change in price, represented by a movement along the same curve; the demand curve itself does not shift.
  • Change in Demand: Caused by factors other than price, resulting in a shift of the entire demand curve—a shift to the right indicates an increase in demand (higher quantity demanded at any given price), and a shift to the left indicates a decrease in demand.

💡 Rule of Thumb: If a change alters the quantity buyers are willing to purchase at any given price, it is a change in demand (curve shift); if it only changes the price itself, it is a change in quantity demanded (movement along the curve).

4.2 Demand Curve

A Demand Schedule is a table listing the quantity demanded at various prices, serving as the data source for the demand curve.

The Demand Curve plots the demand schedule, with price on the vertical axis and quantity on the horizontal axis, appearing as a downward-sloping curve from top-left to bottom-right.

Law of Demand: Under the assumption of Ceteris Paribus (all other things being equal), there is an inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded—as price rises, quantity demanded falls; as price falls, quantity demanded rises.

4.3 Determinants of Demand

The following five categories of factors (mnemonic TONIE) can shift the entire demand curve. Note that this is a primary framework, not an exhaustive list—any factor that changes the quantity buyers are willing to purchase at a given price will cause the curve to shift.

T — Tastes

When consumer preferences for a good change, demand changes accordingly. Increased preference (e.g., advertising effect, social trend) → demand shifts right; decreased preference → demand shifts left.

O — Price of Other Goods

Related goods fall into two categories, with opposite effects:

  • Substitute Goods: Goods that can be used in place of each other; consumers choose between them. Price of a substitute rises → the good in question becomes relatively more attractive → demand for the good shifts right; price of a substitute falls → demand shifts left.
  • Complement Goods: Goods typically consumed together, indispensable to each other. Price of a complement rises → total cost of the combination rises → demand for the good shifts left; price of a complement falls → demand shifts right.

Example: Ebooks and e-readers (Kindle) are complements. If the price of Kindle rises, fewer people buy Kindles, which in turn causes the demand for ebooks to fall, shifting the demand curve left.

N — Number of Buyers

An increase in potential buyers in the market (e.g., population growth, opening of new markets) → quantity demanded at any price increases → demand shifts right; decrease in buyers → demand shifts left.

I — Income

The effect of income on demand depends on the type of good:

  • Normal Good: Income increases → demand increases (shifts right); income decreases → demand decreases (shifts left). Most goods fall into this category; laptops, restaurant dining, etc., are typical examples.
  • Inferior Good: Income increases → demand decreases (shifts left); income decreases → demand increases (shifts right). "Inferior" does not mean poor quality, but rather that when income rises, consumers switch to better substitutes, causing demand for the good to fall. A typical example is the cheapest car on the market—when income increases, people choose better models instead of buying more cheap cars.

E — Expectations

When consumers' expectations about future prices change:

Expectations of future price increases → tendency to buy sooner → current demand shifts right; expectations of future price decreases → tendency to postpone purchases → current demand shifts left.

Example: Consumers of electronics (phones, computers) generally expect prices to continue falling, which to some extent suppresses current demand—people wait for lower prices before buying.

4.4 Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1: Price changes cause changes in demand. Price changes only cause changes in quantity demanded (movement along the curve), not changes in demand itself (no curve shift). Only TONIE factors shift the curve.

Misconception 2: A change in a good's price affects its own demand curve. A change in the price of a good changes its quantity demanded, and the demand curves of related goods (substitutes, complements), but not its own demand curve.

Example: When the price of hot dogs falls, three things happen simultaneously: the quantity demanded of hot dogs increases (movement along its own curve); the demand curve for hot dog buns (complement) shifts right; the demand curve for hamburgers (substitute) shifts left.

Connection to Macroeconomic Frameworks

The equation "Total Spending = Money + Credit" in Dalio's framework can be understood in the context of demand theory as follows: credit expansion is equivalent to an increase in effective income (I in TONIE), pushing the demand curves for normal goods to the right, driving up prices, and creating inflationary pressure during expansionary periods; credit contraction, conversely, shifts demand curves left, reducing price pressure or even leading to deflation. The macroeconomic credit cycle is manifested at the micro level through the simultaneous shifts of countless demand curves in the same direction.

5. Supply

5.1 Supply and Quantity Supplied

Similar to the demand section, the core distinction in supply theory is:

  • Supply is the entire curve, describing all quantities sellers are willing and able to produce at all possible prices—that is, the complete relationship between price and quantity supplied.
  • Quantity Supplied is a specific point on the curve, corresponding to the particular quantity sellers are willing to produce at a given price.

Their mechanisms of change:

  • Change in Quantity Supplied: Caused by a change in price, represented by a movement along the same curve; the supply curve itself does not shift.
  • Change in Supply: Caused by factors other than price, resulting in a shift of the entire supply curve—a shift to the right indicates an increase in supply (higher quantity supplied at any given price), and a shift to the left indicates a decrease in supply.

💡 Rule of Thumb is identical to demand: If a change alters the quantity sellers are willing to produce at any given price, it is a change in supply (curve shift); if it only changes the price itself, it is a change in quantity supplied (movement along the curve).

5.2 Supply Curve

A Supply Schedule is a table listing the quantity supplied at various prices, serving as the data source for the supply curve.

The Supply Curve plots the supply schedule, with price on the vertical axis and quantity on the horizontal axis, and unlike the demand curve, appears as an upward-sloping curve from bottom-left to top-right.

Law of Supply: Under the assumption of Ceteris Paribus (all other things being equal), there is a positive relationship between price and quantity supplied—as price rises, quantity supplied rises; as price falls, quantity supplied falls.

The core difference between the Law of Supply and the Law of Demand: the demand curve slopes downward (negative relationship), while the supply curve slopes upward (positive relationship). This is intuitively understandable: the higher the price, the greater the producer's profit, and the stronger the incentive to produce.

5.3 Determinants of Supply

The following six categories of factors can shift the entire supply curve:

Price of Inputs

Changes in the prices of raw materials, labor, land, energy, and other inputs required to produce a good directly affect production costs.

Input prices rise → production costs rise → profits decrease at any given price → willingness to produce decreases → supply shifts left; Input prices fall → production costs fall → supply shifts right.

Price of Related Goods

⚠️ Note: "Related goods" on the supply side have a different meaning than on the demand side. Here, it refers to production substitutes—other goods that producers can make with the same resources.

If the price of a related good rises → producers shift resources to that good → supply of the good in question shifts left; If the price of a related good falls → supply of the good in question shifts right.

Example: A grape farmer can choose to grow grapes or blueberries on their land. If blueberry prices rise, the farmer will shift more land to blueberries, and grape supply will consequently decrease.

Number of Sellers

An increase in the number of producers in the market → total supply increases (shifts right); decrease in producers → total supply decreases (shifts left). This is a direct aggregate effect at the market level.

Technology

Technological advancements allow for more output with the same inputs, or reduce production costs.

Technological improvement → supply shifts right; technological regression (rare) → supply shifts left.

Government Policy (Government Regulation, Subsidies, Taxes)

  • Government subsidy → lowers production costs → supply shifts right
  • Government tax → raises production costs → supply shifts left
  • Deregulation → supply shifts right; stricter regulation → supply shifts left

Expectations of Future Prices

Similar to the demand side, but with different directional logic:

  • If producers expect future prices to rise → tend to hoard current stock, reduce current sales → current supply shifts left
  • If producers expect future prices to fall → tend to accelerate sales → current supply shifts right

⚠️ This logic only applies to storable goods (e.g., oil, metals). For perishable goods (e.g., grapes), storage is not possible, so price expectations have limited impact on current supply.

The row on price expectations is particularly noteworthy: demand and supply react in opposite directions to the same expectation (future price increase)—consumers buy sooner (demand increases), while producers hoard and withhold sales (supply decreases).

5.5 Common Misconceptions

Misconception: Price increases cause supply to increase.

A price increase only leads to an increase in quantity supplied (movement upward along the curve); the supply curve itself does not change. Only changes in non-price factors cause the entire supply curve to shift (supply increase or decrease).

This is perfectly symmetrical to the misconception in the demand section—price changes only cause changes in "quantity," not changes in the "curve."

Connection to Macroeconomic Frameworks

The supply curve at the macroeconomic level corresponds to the economy's productive capacity. Technological advancement and productivity growth mentioned in Dalio's framework are manifested at the micro level as a continuous rightward shift of the supply curve—more output can be provided at the same price. Imported inflation (e.g., rising oil prices) is a macroeconomic manifestation of rising input prices leading to a leftward shift of the supply curve: the essence of cost-push inflation is that the supply curves of the entire economy simultaneously shift left, pushing up the price level while demand remains unchanged.

6. Market and Equilibrium

6.1 Market Equilibrium

A Market is an interactive mechanism where buyers and sellers exchange goods, services, or resources. When the demand curve and supply curve are plotted on the same graph, their intersection point is the market's Equilibrium.

Equilibrium Price: The price at which quantity demanded equals quantity supplied, also known as the "Market Clearing Price".

Equilibrium Quantity: The actual quantity traded at the equilibrium price.

Meaning of the equilibrium point: At this price, all goods that sellers are willing to sell at this price find buyers, and all buyers willing to purchase at this price find goods. The market clears perfectly, with no surplus and no shortage.

6.2 Disequilibrium: Shortage and Surplus

When the market price deviates from the equilibrium price, the market is in a state of Disequilibrium, manifested as either a shortage or a surplus. Market forces will spontaneously push the price back towards equilibrium.

Shortage: When the price is below the equilibrium price, quantity demanded > quantity supplied.

Price too low → many buyers, few sellers → goods are snapped up, supply cannot meet demand → buyers bid up prices → price rises → quantity supplied increases, quantity demanded decreases → moves towards equilibrium

Surplus: When the price is above the equilibrium price, quantity supplied > quantity demanded.

Price too high → many sellers, few buyers → goods accumulate unsold → sellers are forced to lower prices → quantity demanded increases, quantity supplied decreases → moves towards equilibrium

💡 Core of market mechanism: Shortages push prices up, surpluses push prices down, and both forces point towards the equilibrium point. This is the basic logic of how the price mechanism spontaneously allocates resources.

6.3 Changes in Equilibrium

When the determinants of supply or demand change, the curves shift, and the equilibrium point changes accordingly, forming a new equilibrium price and quantity.

Results of a single curve shift are definite:

ChangeEquilibrium Price P*Equilibrium Quantity Q*
Supply increases (shifts right)Decreases ↓Increases ↑
Supply decreases (shifts left)Increases ↑Decreases ↓
Demand increases (shifts right)Increases ↑Increases ↑
Demand decreases (shifts left)Decreases ↓Decreases ↓

****

When supply and demand change simultaneously, results are partially uncertain:

When both supply and demand change simultaneously, the direction of one variable can be determined, while the other depends on the relative magnitudes of the shifts of the two curves and cannot be determined by direction alone.

Combination of changesEquilibrium Price P*Equilibrium Quantity Q*
Demand increases + Supply increasesUncertain ↕Increases ↑
Demand increases + Supply decreasesIncreases ↑Uncertain ↕
Demand decreases + Supply increasesDecreases ↓Uncertain ↕
Demand decreases + Supply decreasesUncertain ↕Decreases ↓

Rule: When the direction of supply and demand changes is the same, the direction of quantity is certain, and price is uncertain; when the direction of supply and demand changes is opposite, the direction of price is certain, and quantity is uncertain.

Connection to Macroeconomic Frameworks

The market equilibrium mechanism is the microeconomic foundation of the price signaling function in Dalio's framework. Macroeconomic inflation is essentially the result of a large number of markets in the entire economy simultaneously experiencing increased demand (credit expansion pushing demand curves right) or decreased supply (cost shocks pushing supply curves left)—the price increases in each individual market, when aggregated, lead to an increase in the overall price level.

Central banks use interest rate hikes to suppress demand (shifting the demand curve left), precisely leveraging the market equilibrium mechanism to lower price levels. This also explains why monetary policy has limited effectiveness against cost-push inflation—the leftward shift of the supply curve cannot be directly intervened by interest rate hikes.
