Based on Ray Dalio — How the Economic Machine Works (2013) core idea: The economy appears complex, but at its core, it's a machine driven by transactions, which can be understood and predicted.

1. Basic Concepts

1.1 Transactions and Price

The economy is the sum of all transactions. Each transaction consists of a buyer using money or credit to exchange for goods, services, or financial assets from a seller.

Two basic equations:

总支出（Total Spending）= 货币支出 + 信用支出
价格（Price）= 总支出 ÷ 售出的商品总量

💡 One person's spending is another person's income.

This equation is the underlying logic for understanding cycle transmission and crisis spread: any entity reducing spending will directly reduce others' income, and reduced income will trigger further spending cuts, forming a chain reaction.

To understand the price equation within the debt cycle: the total quantity of goods (denominator) changes slowly, so sharp price fluctuations almost entirely come from changes in total spending (numerator), and changes in spending almost entirely come from the expansion and contraction of credit. This is why debt cycles directly manifest as economic booms and busts.

1.2 Money and Credit

Most of what circulates in the economy is not money, but credit.

MoneyCredit
NatureHard currency, immediate settlementPromise to repay, future settlement
Creation MethodOnly central banks can printAny two parties can create it out of thin air
Disappearance MethodRequires active withdrawalCan disappear instantly

Once credit is created, it immediately generates debt: it is an asset for the lender, and a liability for the borrower. When the debt, including principal and interest, is repaid, both this asset and liability disappear, and the transaction ends.

⚠️ Credit can be born instantly and disappear instantly. This is the mechanistic reason why crises come so quickly — it's not that money disappears, but that credit collapses.

The quality of credit depends on its use: credit used for advanced consumption (e.g., buying a TV) cannot generate income to repay debt, which is bad credit; credit used for productive investment (e.g., buying a tractor) can generate income, repay debt, and improve living standards, which is good credit.

Three Ways Credit Disappears

Understanding the mechanism of credit disappearance is crucial for assessing the severity of a crisis.

  1. Normal Repayment: The borrower repays the principal and interest on time, the asset and liability naturally disappear, with no negative impact on the economy.
  2. Default: The borrower cannot repay, and the lender's "asset" shrinks or becomes zero.
  3. Voluntary Contraction due to Confidence Collapse: This is the most impactful and easily overlooked type during a crisis. No one needs to actually default — as long as lenders start worrying that borrowers might not be able to repay, they will stop new lending; borrowers, due to uncertain prospects, will also voluntarily stop taking on new debt. The rate of new credit creation falls to zero or even negative, while old credit is gradually repaid, and the total amount of credit rapidly shrinks.

During a crisis, three directions worsen simultaneously:

  • Newly created credit → approaches zero
  • Normally repaid credit → continuously consumed
  • Defaulted credit disappearing → begins to increase

Total credit = new creation - normal repayment - default disappearance. All three worsen simultaneously, leading to extremely rapid contraction.

1.3 Nominal vs. Real Values

When analyzing economic data, two perspectives must be distinguished:

  • Nominal Value = book value, not considering inflation
  • Real Value ≈ Nominal Value - Inflation Rate, reflecting true purchasing power

When it comes to income, spending, and debt burden, nominal values are used to describe mechanisms and transmission (debt contracts are written in nominal figures; if you borrow 1 million, you must repay 1 million, regardless of inflation); real values are used to assess actual living standards and debt pressure.

Example 1: Nominal Wage vs. Real Wage

SituationNominal Wage IncreaseInflation RateReal Wage Change
Appears to have a raise+3%+5%-2% (actually poorer)
Appears no raise0%-2% (deflation)+2% (actually richer)
True Growth+5%+2%+3%

Example 2: Asymmetric Effect of Inflation on Debt

Inflation has an asymmetric effect on debtors and creditors:

  • Inflation is beneficial to debtors: repaying debt with "cheaper money," actual debt burden decreases
  • Deflation is a disaster for debtors: nominal income decreases, but nominal debt remains unchanged, so the actual debt burden increases instead

This is the fundamental reason why central banks choose to create moderate inflation during deleveraging — quietly diluting the real value of society's debt.

1.4 Five Economic Entities

All economic activities are completed by five types of entities through division of labor. Understanding the function of each entity is necessary to comprehend "who is doing what" in the cycle.

EntityCore Function
Individuals/HouseholdsConsumption, borrowing and repaying loans, are the basic units of credit expansion and contraction
BusinessesInvestment, employment, production, amplifying cyclical fluctuations
Commercial BanksDecide whether to lend, are the direct channel for credit creation, and are also damaged in crises
Central GovernmentTax, spend, issue debt, needs to expand deficits to inject demand during recessions
Central BankRegulate interest rates, control money supply, print money to buy assets when necessary

⚠️ Only the central bank has the right to "print money." Commercial banks can only decide whether to lend, they cannot create base money.

The credit chain among these five entities is: Central Bank sets interest rates (regulating the cost of credit) → Commercial Banks decide to lend (creating credit) → Individuals and Businesses borrow for consumption and investment (using credit) → Central Government directly intervenes when the credit system fails (replacing credit).

Who is the Main Actor in Different Stages

StageMain ActorsWhat they do
Expansion PhaseIndividuals, Businesses, Commercial BanksBorrowing, consumption, investment, lending
Inflation OverheatingCentral BankRaise interest rates, suppress lending
RecessionIndividuals, BusinessesForced to cut spending and repay debt
Early DeleveragingIndividuals, Businesses, Commercial BanksTightening, default, stopping lending
Mid-DeleveragingCentral GovernmentExpand deficits, launch stimulus plans
Mid-DeleveragingCentral BankPrint money, buy government bonds

The danger of multiple entities contracting simultaneously: The severity of the 2008 crisis partly stemmed from simultaneous contraction on three levels — individuals repaying loans, companies laying off staff, and banks being reluctant to lend. The equation "one person's spending is another person's income" had its greatest destructive power here: when all entities simultaneously reduce spending, no one's income can remain constant, and the entire economy enters a self-reinforcing downward spiral.

2. How the Economy Grows

2.1 Three Major Driving Forces

There are only three forces driving the economy. By superimposing them, you get a practical framework for determining the economy's current position:

实际经济水平 = 生产力增长趋势线
            + 短期债务周期波动（5–8年）
            + 长期债务周期波动（75–100年）
Driving ForceTime SpanDriving FactorsWho Regulates
Productivity GrowthLong-term linear increaseTechnology, education, innovationCannot be directly regulated
Short-Term Debt Cycle5–8 yearsCredit expansion and contractionCentral Bank (interest rates)
Long-Term Debt Cycle75–100 yearsLong-term debt accumulation to structural limitsLimited, tools fail at critical point

The "economic expansion" and "recession" felt daily correspond to the upward and downward phases of the short-term debt cycle.

2.2 Productivity Growth

Productivity growth is the sole source of true wealth. Diligence, innovation, and knowledge accumulation continuously improve living standards, which cannot be replaced by borrowing.

However, productivity growth is slow and stable, with little short-term fluctuation, thus it is not the main driver of short-term economic fluctuations. Short-term fluctuations are primarily credit-driven.

Investment implication: Debt-driven growth has a ceiling; only a genuine increase in productivity can continuously raise living standards.

2.3 The Essence of Lending

Lending = bringing future purchasing power forward to be used now.

Borrowing means consuming more than income now, and in the future, one must consume less than income to repay debt. This naturally forms a cycle. Borrowing at any time initiates a mechanical, predictable chain of future events — which is the fundamental reason for the existence of short-term and long-term debt cycles.

How much one can borrow depends on creditworthiness, which consists of two conditions:

  • Ability to Repay: The level of income relative to debt
  • Collateral: Assets that can be used to repay debt in case of non-repayment

3. Short-Term Debt Cycle

The short-term debt cycle (5–8 years) is regulated by the central bank through interest rates. As long as there is room for interest rate adjustment, this cycle can be managed.

  1. Expansion Phase
    Easy access to credit → Increased spending (nominal terms) → Increased income → Rising prices → Inflation
    The expansion phase has a self-reinforcing cycle: increased credit → increased spending → increased income for others → improved creditworthiness → ability to borrow more → further increased spending. This positive feedback (Self-Reinforcing Pattern) continuously drives economic expansion.
  2. Central Bank Tightening Phase
    Central bank does not want inflation to be too high → Raises interest rates → Cost of new borrowing increases; monthly payments for floating-rate debt increase → Lending willingness decreases, spending of some debtors is compressed → Income decreases
  3. Recession Phase
    Sustained decrease in spending → Reduced upward pressure on prices (deflation occurs in severe cases) → Decreased economic activity
  4. Central Bank Easing Phase
    Recession is too severe → Central bank lowers interest rates → Lending willingness recovers (premise: debt burden has not reached structural limits) → Spending recovers → New round of expansion

📌 At the end of each short-term cycle, the total debt is higher than in the previous cycle — because people are inherently inclined to borrow more and repay less. This is the structural source of long-term debt cycle accumulation.

3.1 Inflation and Deflation

  • Inflation: Decreased purchasing power of money, sustained increase in overall price levels.
  • Deflation: Increased purchasing power of money, sustained decrease in overall price levels.

Note the word "sustained" — individual commodity price fluctuations are not inflation/deflation; it refers to a systemic change in the overall price level.

Causes of Inflation

价格 = (信用 + 货币) ÷ 售出的商品总量
  • Demand-Pull: Credit expansion → Total spending exceeds production capacity → Numerator (credit) increases → Prices rise. This is the main character in Dalio's framework.
  • Cost-Push: Rising production costs → Companies cannot maintain original output at the same price, raise prices to pass on costs → Denominator shrinks → Prices rise. This type of inflation is not accompanied by increased demand, but rather compresses consumption, leading to a decrease in real purchasing power.
  • Monetary Over-issuance: Money supply growth far exceeds actual output growth → Numerator (money) increases → Prices rise. A typical mechanism for hyperinflation, Germany in the 1920s is a textbook example.

Causes of Deflation

价格 = (信用 + 货币) ÷ 售出的商品总量
  • Credit Collapse (most dangerous): During deleveraging, a large amount of credit instantly disappears → Total spending plummets → Prices plummet, extremely fast, self-reinforcing.
  • Productivity Improvement (benign): Technological progress improves production efficiency → Supply increases → Prices naturally fall. This type of deflation is not a bad thing. Example: Over the past few decades, electronic products have continuously decreased in price, backed by advancements in semiconductor technology, improving living standards.
  • Long-term Demand Contraction: Aging population, long-term low consumption willingness → Sustained insufficient demand → Decreased corporate income, low investment willingness → Shrinking lending demand, reduced credit creation → Contraction of total money/credit in circulation → Further decrease in total spending → Downward prices → Deflationary expectations form → Consumption further postponed. Japan is a typical example.
Good DeflationBad Deflation
CauseIncreased productivity, increased supplyCredit collapse or long-term demand contraction
ResultImproved living standardsEconomic downward spiral
Needs intervention?NoYes

Common Indicators

IndicatorMeasuresDescription

Producer Price Index; PPIChange in prices of raw materials and intermediate goods in the production processLeading indicator for CPI

PPI leads CPI: Rising production costs often take a few months to transmit to consumer prices. Looking at PPI can help predict CPI trends in advance.

3.2 The Central Bank's Toolkit

Tool One: Interest Rate Regulation

Interest rate is the price of borrowing money. Adjusting interest rates is equivalent to adjusting the cost of borrowing, which in turn affects borrowing willingness, and ultimately affects total spending.

Transmission chain: Interest rate cut → Borrowing becomes cheaper → Borrowing willingness increases → Spending increases → Economic expansion

Tool Two: Quantitative Easing (QE)

When interest rates have fallen to extremely low levels and traditional tools become ineffective, the central bank needs to switch to another set of tools.

The name QE comes from the Japanese 量的緩和 (ryōteki kanwa), first pioneered by the Bank of Japan in 2001. Ordinary interest rate cuts adjust the price of borrowing money (interest rates), while QE adjusts the quantity of money supply (directly expanding the central bank's balance sheet) — switching from "price regulation" to "quantity regulation," which is the meaning of "quantitative."

How QE works: The central bank directly buys assets (mainly government bonds, mortgage-backed securities) in the open market → Injects money into the banking system → Banks have more funds to lend → Simultaneously pushes down long-term interest rates.

Common Limitations of Both Tools

Both sets of tools rely on banks being willing to lend, and businesses and individuals being willing to borrow. If the economy is in a deleveraging phase, and everyone wants to repay debt rather than borrow, no matter how low interest rates are or how abundant money is in banks, the transmission chain will break at the "willingness to borrow" link.

Another important boundary: the central bank can only purchase financial assets, it cannot directly distribute money to ordinary people. Therefore, QE money first enters financial markets, pushing up stock, bond, and real estate prices. Groups holding these assets (usually the wealthy) benefit more, while the consumer side feels limited impact. This is the mechanistic reason why QE after 2008 pushed up asset prices, but wealth inequality simultaneously widened.

Why Central Banks Aim for an Inflation Target of Around 2%

Harms of excessively high inflation:

  1. Erodes purchasing power, especially harming fixed-income groups
  2. Distorted price signals, decreased efficiency of resource allocation
  3. Systematically penalizes savings, encourages excessive borrowing
  4. Inflation has self-reinforcing characteristics; once out of control, it enters hyperinflation

Harms of excessively low inflation (deflation):

  • Triggers a "postponed consumption" mentality — consumption delayed → corporate income decreases → layoffs → fewer people consume → prices continue to fall. This is a deflationary spiral, in which Japan was deeply mired in the 1990s.

2% is a buffer between two extremes: low enough not to distort the economy; high enough to preserve room for monetary policy adjustment (interest rates won't easily be forced to zero), and simultaneously provide debtors with breathing room.

The Dilemma of Imported Inflation

Imported Inflation refers to inflation "imported" from abroad — domestic monetary policy and debt cycles are normal, but rising import prices lead to a passive increase in the overall domestic price level.

Trigger mechanism: Rising commodity prices (e.g., oil, food) or currency depreciation → Increased import costs → Companies raise prices to pass on costs → Overall price level passively increases, unrelated to domestic demand.

This puts central banks in a dilemma: the tool to combat inflation is raising interest rates (suppressing domestic credit expansion), but the root cause of imported inflation is not domestic credit. Raising interest rates would only suppress borrowing and consumption, push up unemployment, while import prices would still rise, and inflation would not disappear. This state is called Stagflation — economic stagnation + inflation occurring simultaneously, which is the most difficult scenario for central bank tools to handle. The 1970s US oil crisis is a textbook example.

4. Long-Term Debt Cycle

The long-term debt cycle is the longest (75–100 years) and most destructive force. It is not a simple amplified version of the short-term debt cycle, but rather the structural result of dozens of short-term cycles superimposed and accumulated. Understanding it requires grasping four consecutive stages: leverage accumulation → peak → deleveraging → reflexive recovery.

4.1 Leverage Accumulation Phase

Structural Starting Point: Each Short-Term Cycle Leaves More Debt

At the end of the short-term debt cycle, the central bank stimulates a new round of expansion by cutting interest rates. However, at the start of each expansion, the total debt is slightly higher than in the previous round — because people are inherently inclined to borrow more and repay less, and the troughs of short-term cycles never fully clear the debt accumulated from the previous round. After dozens of short-term cycles, this "leaving a little each time" effect accumulates into a massive debt stock.

Core Mechanism of Self-Reinforcement: Positive Feedback between Collateral and Credit

Increased borrowing → Increased demand for asset purchases (stocks, real estate) → Rising asset prices → Increased collateral value → Improved creditworthiness → Ability to borrow more → Further rising asset prices → ……

This cycle can sustain itself for a long time. As long as asset prices are rising, the debt burden appears manageable — net worth (assets minus liabilities) increases, and people feel increasingly wealthy. This is why bubbles can last for decades.

Core Indicator for Measuring Debt Pressure

债务负担（Debt Burden）= 债务还款额 ÷ 收入

Note that "repayment amount" is used here, not "total debt" — the real pressure comes not from the stock of debt, but from the proportion of income that must be repaid each period. As long as income growth can cover repayment growth, this cycle can continue.

However, debt accumulation has an inherent asymmetry: borrowing can be done in a day, but repayment takes years; asset prices rise quickly during credit expansion, but fall quickly once reversed. This dictates that the long-term debt cycle must have a peak.

4.2 Long-Term Debt Peak

Trigger Condition: Repayment Speed Exceeds Income Growth Rate

The Long-Term Debt Peak is not caused by a single sudden event, but rather the natural eruption of internal pressure after debt accumulates to a certain critical point:

Debt repayment growth rate > Income growth rate → Net decrease in money available for consumption and investment → Total spending (nominal terms) decreases → Others' income decreases → Creditworthiness declines → Lending capacity decreases → Asset prices begin to fall → Collateral value shrinks → Creditworthiness further deteriorates → Cycle reverses

Early Signals of the Peak Phase

  • Asset prices stop rising, trading volume shrinks
  • Credit standards quietly tighten, marginal borrowers begin to be rejected for loans
  • Debt repayment as a percentage of income reaches historical highs
  • Nominal income growth rate begins to fall below debt interest rates

The characteristic of the peak is: even without external shocks, internal pressure is already sufficient to trigger a reversal. External shocks (such as central bank interest rate hikes, market panic) often merely ignite the fuse, rather than creating the problem.

Historical references: US 1929, Japan 1989, US 2008.

4.3 Deleveraging

Essential Difference from Recession

RecessionDeleveraging
Trigger CauseShort-term credit tighteningDebt burden accumulated to structural limits
Interest Rate LevelStill room for reductionExtremely low or even zero, stimulus effect ineffective
Problem EssenceBorrowing costs too highTotal debt itself too heavy, borrowers do not want to borrow more
Central Bank ToolsInterest rate cuts effectiveInterest rate cuts ineffective
MetaphorFever, fever reducer worksOrgans starting to fail, fever reducer not enough

Interest rate cuts are effective during a recession because the problem lies in borrowing costs. During deleveraging, interest rate cuts are ineffective because the problem is not the cost, but that debtors no longer want to take on new debt — their primary task is to repay old debts and repair their balance sheets. No matter how low interest rates are, they cannot change the reality of "debt must be repaid."

Typical Symptoms of Deleveraging

During the deleveraging phase, multiple negative feedback loops start simultaneously and reinforce each other:

  • Spending decreases → Income decreases → Creditworthiness declines → Lending decreases → Spending further decreases
  • Meanwhile: Asset prices plummet → Collateral value shrinks → Bank balance sheets deteriorate → Banks tighten lending → More borrowers unable to refinance → Forced to sell assets → Prices further fall
  • Societal level: Rising unemployment → Intensified social conflicts → Rising extreme political risks

Four Responses

There are no shortcuts to deleveraging. The essence of the problem is that the total debt exceeds the economy's capacity to digest it, and can only be reduced through the following four methods:

Economic Effect
Spending Cuts (Austerity)Deflationary
Debt RestructuringDeflationary
Wealth Redistribution (Taxing the rich)Deflationary
Central Bank Printing MoneyInflationary
  1. The Paradox of Austerity: Spending cuts trigger the reverse chain of "one person's spending is another's income" A cuts spending → B's income decreases (nominal) → B is also forced to cut → C's income decreases → …… When the entire society cuts simultaneously, income falls faster than debt repayment, and the debt burden (Debt ÷ Income) actually rises. This stage can lead to an economic depression.
  2. The Essence of Debt Restructuring: Lenders accept principal write-downs, extended maturities, or lower interest rates in exchange for partial recovery (A little of something is better than all of nothing). However, debt restructuring is also deflationary: while debt is extinguished, corresponding assets are also extinguished, total purchasing power further shrinks, potentially triggering bank runs.
  3. The Political Difficulty of Wealth Redistribution: Taxing the wealthy transfers resources to low- and middle-income groups, but under the social pressures of deleveraging, political friction is immense. Mishandling can lead to the rise of extreme politics — 1930s Germany is an example.
  4. The Limits of Central Bank Money Printing: The central bank can only purchase financial assets and cannot directly distribute money to ordinary people. Therefore, it must cooperate with the central government. Central bank buys government bonds → Government financing costs decrease, allowing for expanded budget deficits → Money injected into the real economy through stimulus plans and unemployment benefits → Nominal household income rises → Debt burden relatively decreases ⚠️ Excessive money printing → Hyperinflation. Germany in the 1920s is a textbook counter-example.

Beautiful Deleveraging

Deflationary tools and inflationary tools are properly balanced, with the two forces offsetting each other → Debt slowly declines relative to income, real economy experiences moderate positive growth, and inflation does not get out of control. This is "Beautiful Deleveraging."

Mathematical condition for achievement:

名义收入增速 > 债务的名义利率

Example: Debt/income ratio is 100%, interest rate is 2%. If nominal income growth is only 1%, the debt burden continuously rises, and deleveraging fails. If nominal income growth reaches 4% (2% from real economic growth, 2% from moderate inflation created by money printing), the debt burden begins to actually decrease.

Key premise: Money printing does not necessarily cause inflation — if the new money merely fills the void left by disappearing credit, and total spending (nominal terms) remains unchanged, prices will not rise. Total spending is the determinant of prices, regardless of whether the spending comes from money or credit.

Complete Cycle Timeline

  • Phase One: Deflationary Spiral (most painful)
    • Asset prices plummet → Credit disappears → Austerity + debt restructuring occur simultaneously
    • Nominal income falls faster than debt repayment → Debt burden worsens
    • Unemployment soars, social conflicts intensify
  • Phase Two: Policy Intervention
    • Central bank cuts interest rates to extremely low levels (already ineffective) → Initiates QE
    • Central government expands fiscal deficit, directly injects demand into the economy
  • Phase Three: Equilibrium Point
    • Deflationary forces and inflationary forces reach equilibrium
    • Nominal income begins to grow, debt burden begins to actually decrease
  • Phase Four: Reflation
    • Income rises → Creditworthiness improves → Lending cautiously resumes
    • Moderate economic growth, but growth rate is much lower than before deleveraging
    • Asset prices slowly recover, often taking longer than the economy

Full duration: Over 10 years

The reflation phase is the sign that the cycle has truly completed its loop — it's not that debt has disappeared, but that the debt burden (debt ÷ income) has returned to a sustainable level, and the economy can re-expand on a new, lower leverage ratio. During this phase, interest rates typically need to remain low to ensure that nominal income growth consistently exceeds debt interest rates.

Two Extremes of Imbalanced Allocation:

  • Deflationary forces too strong (austerity-led, insufficient money printing) → Depression: Economy continuously contracts, actual debt burden becomes heavier and heavier, falling into a deflationary spiral.
  • Inflationary forces too strong (excessive money printing, lack of restraint) → Hyperinflation: Currency and credit collapse, prices spiral out of control.

5. Limits of the Framework

The core assumption of Dalio's framework is that economic problems originate internally — from excessive credit expansion and contraction. However, in reality, several types of external variables can undermine this assumption, rendering parts or all of the framework's tools ineffective.

5.1 Exogenous Supply-Side Shocks

Supply-side shocks such as wars, natural disasters, and trade blockades directly compress the denominator of the price equation (effective supply), causing inflation. However, this is unrelated to the credit cycle, and the central bank's interest rate hike tools have limited effectiveness.

Example: In February 2026, US-Israeli military strikes on Iran led to the blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, affecting approximately 20% of global oil supply. This created an imported inflation shock for all energy-importing economies, particularly devastating Japan (which relies on the Middle East for about 90% of its crude oil). The International Energy Agency characterized this as the largest global energy supply disruption in history.

The result is often stagflation — central banks face the dilemma of "raising interest rates to curb inflation but harming growth, or not raising rates and letting inflation spiral out of control," with no clean solution.

5.2 Demographic Structure

Population aging leads to structural demand deficiency: older people have low consumption willingness, and medical and pension expenditures crowd out other consumption; a shrinking working population limits the growth of the economy's actual production capacity; fewer younger generations lead to long-term pessimistic expectations for future demand among businesses, deterring investment.

This is where monetary and fiscal policies cannot reach. The Bank of Japan has maintained ultra-loose policies for a long time, but inflation simply won't pick up — there is a persistent structural leak on the demand side, offsetting the effects created by monetary easing.

5.3 Changes in Corporate Behavior

After a bubble bursts, companies' primary goal shifts from profit maximization to debt minimization. Even if interest rates fall to zero, companies will not borrow for investment, because repairing balance sheets damaged by excessive leverage is the first priority.

This breaks the monetary policy transmission chain: central bank easing → banks have money → but companies don't borrow → the real economy doesn't feel it.

Japanese listed companies have long hoarded large amounts of cash, not investing, not distributing dividends, and not raising salaries. Funds are locked up and do not participate in the economic cycle. This is one of the core mechanisms behind the long-term ineffectiveness of Japan's macro-easing policies.

5.4 Lack of Structural Reforms

Productivity growth is the sole source of long-term wealth. However, productivity growth requires structural reforms: labor market flexibility, a competitive business environment, and an innovation ecosystem. These changes proceed extremely slowly due to political and socio-cultural resistance, and are far from being replaceable by monetary and fiscal policies.

During Japan's lost three decades, lifetime employment protected jobs but hindered talent mobility; cross-shareholding reduced competitive pressure; and the internet era failed to foster a new generation of tech companies. Structural issues are the deepest constraints.

6. Application

6.1 Three Principles of Action

At the end of the video, Dalio provides three principles, applicable to individuals and policymakers alike. There is an inherent logical hierarchy among the three:

Principle Three is Fundamental: Do everything possible to increase productivity — productivity growth is the sole source of true wealth, and in the long run, this is the most important thing.

Principle One is the Prerequisite for Protecting the Fundamental: Do not let debt grow faster than income — an excessive debt burden will crush the space for productivity accumulation, leading the economy into deleveraging rather than growth.

Principle Two is to Prevent False Prosperity: Do not let income grow faster than productivity — income that deviates from productivity for too long means debt or bubbles are driving growth, which will eventually lead to a loss of competitiveness and trigger adjustments.

The logical relationship of the three principles: increasing productivity is the goal, controlling debt is to ensure the goal is not undermined, and preventing false income is to avoid going down the wrong path.

6.2 Determining Current Cycle Position

The core value of this framework for investment is not to predict short-term market trends, but to help determine the current macroeconomic stage, avoiding inverse decisions at the wrong cycle position.

Judgment Process

Step One: Observe interest rate trends, qualitatively determine the cycle stage

  • Interest rates rising → Central bank tightening, in late expansion or overheating phase
  • Interest rates falling → Central bank easing, in recession or early deleveraging phase
  • Interest rates have bottomed out, tools ineffective → Possibly in deleveraging phase, proceed to step two for confirmation Step Two: Look at real wages, confirm transmission direction
  • Nominal wage growth > Inflation rate → Real purchasing power is improving, credit expansion is effective
  • Nominal wage growth < Inflation rate → Real purchasing power is declining, even if nominal figures look good, it does not represent real improvement Step Three: Look at debt burden, determine if it's a short-term or long-term problem
  • Debt/GDP is within historical normal range and trend is stable → Short-term debt cycle problem, interest rate cuts and other tools are effective
  • Debt/GDP continuously rising and at historical highs → Long-term debt cycle pressure, need to be wary of deleveraging risk Step Four: Compare against framework limits, check if tools will fail
  • Are there exogenous supply-side shocks (energy, food)?
  • Does demographic structure cause a long-term demand gap?
  • Are companies hoarding cash instead of investing?

Key Indicator References

  • Interest rate levels and trends (hikes/cuts/bottoming out)
  • Inflation data (CPI/PCE, whether it exceeds target)
  • Real wage changes (nominal wage growth - inflation rate)
  • Debt burden (Debt/GDP, historical position and trend)
  • Asset price trends (whether in a self-reinforcing cycle)
  • Central bank policy signals and rhetoric

📌 Limitations of this framework: It can help you understand where you are currently, and why certain policies occur, but it cannot precisely predict short-term market trends. Macroeconomic judgment is context, not a timing tool.

7. Case Study

This section uses the theoretical framework as a basis to test its explanatory power through specific historical cases. The value of the cases lies not in memorizing events, but in understanding: how the same framework logic produced distinctly different results in different countries and different eras.

7.1 Japan 1989: Anatomy of a Bubble

The Beginning of the Bubble

Japan's 1989 bubble originated from a geopolitical decision in 1985.

Plaza Accord (September 1985)

The US external trade deficit continued to widen, and the Reagan administration attributed the cause to an overvalued dollar. In September 1985, the United States, Japan, UK, France, and West Germany signed an agreement at the Plaza Hotel in New York to depress the dollar through coordinated foreign exchange intervention.

When the agreement was signed, the USD to JPY exchange rate was approximately 238:1. Over the next two years, the yen appreciated sharply, reaching 127 at one point in 1987, an appreciation of nearly double.

The direct impact of the yen's appreciation was a collapse in export competitiveness — Japan's exports to the US fell to about half of their 1986 peak by 1992. This recession, triggered by the exchange rate, is known in Japanese as "円高不況" (endaka fukyō, strong yen recession).

Japan's Response: Aggressive Interest Rate Cuts

To counter the damage to exports, the Bank of Japan (BOJ) continuously cut interest rates starting in 1986, lowering the benchmark discount rate from 5% to 2.5% in February 1987 — a post-war historical low.

The critical policy error was that this extremely low interest rate was maintained for over two full years (February 1987 to May 1989), far exceeding the time needed to address the strong yen recession. In fact, the Japanese economy had already entered expansion in the second half of 1987, but the BOJ, citing international policy coordination (Louvre Accord) and external pressure, was slow to tighten monetary policy.

Mechanism of the Bubble

In the mature Japanese economy, extremely low interest rates did not flow into new factories and productive investments, but rather into asset markets. This triggered the positive feedback loop described in 4.1:

Increased borrowing → Increased demand for asset purchases (stocks, real estate) → Rising asset prices → Increased collateral value → Ability to borrow more → Asset prices further rise → ……

The Bank of Japan also further accelerated credit expansion through its "Window Guidance" (窓口指導) mechanism, directly allocating credit quotas to commercial banks. Between 1988 and 1989, the growth rate of funds in the corporate and household sectors approached an annual year-on-year increase of 14%.

Figures at the Peak of the Bubble

  • The Nikkei index rose from approximately 13,000 points in 1985 to nearly 38,915 points by the end of 1989, a nearly threefold increase in five years.
  • The theoretical market value of land around the Tokyo Imperial Palace was once estimated to be enough to buy the entire state of California, USA.
  • Japanese companies engaged in large-scale global acquisitions: Mitsubishi Estate acquired New York's Rockefeller Center in 1989, and Japanese companies bought large amounts of assets in Hawaii and across the United States.

This is a typical form of the long-term debt cycle accumulation phase in Dalio's framework — the debt burden appears manageable because asset prices rise simultaneously; everyone feels increasingly wealthy. This is why bubbles can last for decades.

Collapse: Interest Rate Hikes Begin in 1989

The Bank of Japan finally recognized the asset bubble risk in 1989 and began a series of interest rate hikes, raising the discount rate from 2.5% to 6% by August 1990.

Bank of Japan begins raising interest rates → Borrowing costs rise → Asset prices begin to fall → Collateral value shrinks → Banks begin to tighten lending → Forced asset sales → Prices further fall.

By 1992, Japan's asset bubble had completely burst. The stock market fell by about 60% within three years of its peak. Land prices began to collapse in 1991, with the average price of commercial, residential, and industrial land in the six major cities falling by 15.5% that year, and continuing to decline for nearly two decades thereafter.

This was the landmark moment of the long-term debt peak. External shocks (interest rate hikes) merely ignited the fuse; the internal unsustainability of the bubble had already taken shape.

The Lost Three Decades: Why Not Ten Years

Dalio's framework predicts that deleveraging takes about 10 years. Japan took thirty years, for multi-layered reasons.

Surface Level: Policy Errors

① Zombie Banks not cleared for a long time

After the bubble burst, the Japanese banking system accumulated a large amount of non-performing loans on its books, but banks were unwilling to admit it — admission would mean insufficient capital and the risk of forced restructuring. The result was that banks continuously injected funds into insolvent "zombie companies," maintaining their superficial existence, a practice known as "evergreening."

Research found that in the early 2000s, approximately 30% of all listed manufacturing, construction, real estate, retail, wholesale, and service companies in Japan were relying on bank transfusions to stay alive.

These zombie banks tied up significant credit resources but failed to drive genuine economic recovery — they used their limited lending capacity to sustain companies that had no vitality, rather than supporting new industries with potential.

② Intermittent fiscal stimulus, limited effectiveness

Throughout the 1990s, the Japanese government launched multiple rounds of fiscal stimulus packages, totaling hundreds of billions of dollars. However, each time stimulus initially showed results, the government tightened policies due to concerns about fiscal deficits, leading to repeated abortive recoveries.

③ Poor timing for 1997 consumption tax hike

In 1997, the Japanese government, to consolidate public finances, raised the consumption tax from 3% to 5%. The timing was chosen just as the Asian financial crisis erupted and the Japanese economy had shown a slight sign of recovery — this tax hike directly suppressed demand, and Japan fell back into recession that year, with the banking sector subsequently experiencing a systemic crisis.

Mid-Level: Deflationary Expectations Locked In

Under deflationary expectations, the value of cash increases over time. In this situation, Japanese companies began cutting wages, R&D, and other investments, opting to hold cash.

This created a self-reinforcing cycle: consumers expect "it will be cheaper tomorrow, so don't buy today" → corporate income falls, companies dare not raise wages or invest → wages don't rise, consumption further shrinks → prices continue to fall → expectations further solidify.

Once deflationary expectations are embedded in the behavior of economic agents, monetary policy loses its transmission channel. Even if interest rates fall to zero, borrowing willingness will not recover — this is precisely the mechanism described by the limitations of the 3.3 toolkit.

In the long run, Japan's per capita GDP still maintained growth, and the unemployment rate remained at a low level — this indicates that the "lost three decades" is a relative concept: Japan's performance severely lagged compared to its potential growth and compared to other developed countries during the same period. But it was not a complete economic collapse.

7.2 US 2008: How Financial Innovation Severed Risk Constraints

Mechanism of the Bubble: Systemic Failure of Risk Transfer

The 2008 crisis in the US had a fundamental structural difference from Japan in 1989: Japan experienced a traditional bank credit bubble, while the US saw an accumulation of systemic risk created by financial innovation.

Starting Point: Low Interest Rates After the 2000 Dot-Com Bubble Burst

The dot-com bubble burst in 2000, coupled with the impact of the 9/11 attacks in 2001, led the Federal Reserve to continuously lower the federal funds rate from 6.5% to 1% by 2003. This extremely low interest rate environment persisted for a relatively long time, causing large amounts of capital to seek higher returns and flow into the real estate market.

Key Accelerator: Securitization Severed Risk Constraints

Traditional bank lending model: Bank lends → Borrower repays → Bank bears risk, thus has incentive to prudently screen borrowers.

New securitization model: Bank lends → Packages loans into Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS) → Sells to investors → Risk transferred out → Bank can continue to lend more, credit standards continuously lowered

The securitization rate of subprime mortgages rose from 54% in 2001 to 75% in 2006. Concurrently, the proportion of subprime mortgages in total loan originations increased from less than 10% in 2001–2003 to 18–20% between 2004–2006.

By mid-2008, over 60% of US housing mortgages had been securitized — aggregated into mortgage-backed securities, whose income streams were "tranched," providing higher-risk income streams to some investors and lower-risk income streams to others.

This mechanism created severe moral hazard: loan originators no longer needed to care whether borrowers could repay, because loans would be immediately packaged and sold, with the risk borne by investors in the market. The result was a continuous decline in credit standards, and more and more borrowers who were originally unable to repay received loans.

Regulatory Vacuum

Investment banks and hedge funds, part of the "Shadow Banking System," played an increasingly important role but were not subject to the same regulatory constraints as deposit-taking banks. These institutions used complex off-balance-sheet derivatives and securitization methods to conceal their true risk exposure from investors and regulators.

Collapse: Interest Rate Hikes from 2004 Trigger Default Chain

The Federal Reserve began raising interest rates in 2004, increasing the federal funds rate from 1% to 5.25–5.5% by June 2006.

This directly hit a large number of subprime borrowers holding Adjustable Rate Mortgages (ARM) — they took out loans at extremely low "teaser rates" during low-interest periods, and after interest rate hikes, their monthly payments surged, making them unable to repay.

Default chain: Large-scale defaults by subprime borrowers → MBS values collapse → Financial institutions holding MBS suffer balance sheet damage → Institutions distrust each other (no one knows how many bad assets others hold) → Interbank market freezes → Credit system paralyzes.

On September 15, 2008, Lehman Brothers filed for bankruptcy, becoming the landmark event of the full-blown crisis. This was an extreme case of the "third way" credit disappears as described in 1.3 — not due to massive defaults, but due to a systemic collapse of confidence, with credit across the entire financial system evaporating within weeks.

Recovery: Why Faster Than Japan

The US deleveraging process avoided Japan's mistakes at several critical junctures.

① Rapidly acknowledge bad debts, force banks to clear them

The Federal Reserve and Treasury Department forced major banks to disclose capital shortfalls through "Stress Tests," injecting capital where needed and restructuring where necessary. This avoided the Japanese-style "zombie banks" dragging down the credit system for a long time.

② Rapid, large-scale QE by the Federal Reserve

The Federal Reserve cut interest rates to near zero by the end of 2008 and immediately launched large-scale quantitative easing: the first round of QE purchased approximately $1.75 trillion in assets, with subsequent two rounds totaling trillions, far exceeding the speed and scale of the Bank of Japan's actions back then.

③ Sustained and sufficient fiscal stimulus

In 2009, the Obama administration launched the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act (ARRA) of approximately $787 billion, directly injecting demand into the economy, with coordinated fiscal and monetary policies.

④ Learned from Japan's 1997 tax hike lesson, did not tighten too early

The US did not rush to cut its fiscal deficit in the early stages of recovery, allowing deficits to remain high for a longer period until the economy found its footing.

Core Lessons

The essential difference between the two crises was not the size of the bubble, but the policy mix for deleveraging. Japan's deflationary forces (austerity + uncleared bad debts) long outweighed inflationary forces (monetary stimulus), and the mathematical condition for beautiful deleveraging (nominal income growth > debt interest rate) was never met; the US after 2008, through large-scale QE and fiscal stimulus, achieved a closer balance in its mix, coupled with rapid clearing of the banking system, which shortened the recovery time.

7.3 Post-COVID Status of Three Countries (2025–2026)

The COVID-19 pandemic was an exogenous shock, different from a credit cycle naturally peaking, but the way governments responded directly determined subsequent macroeconomic trends. The following analysis is based on the latest data as of April 2026.

United States: After a Soft Landing, New Uncertainties

Policies During the Pandemic: Direct Payments to Residents

The key difference between the US pandemic response and 2008 was the flow of money. QE in 2008 involved money entering the financial system, then expected to transmit to the real economy through banks. During the pandemic, the US government directly issued cash checks (stimulus checks) to residents, coupled with large-scale unemployment benefits, bypassing the banking transmission link and directly driving a rebound in demand.

This led to the inflation surge in 2021–2022: demand-side stimulus combined with supply chain disruptions (numerator increased, denominator decreased), with US CPI peaking at approximately 9% in mid-2022, the highest in forty years.

The Federal Reserve's Aggressive Interest Rate Hikes

To curb inflation, the Federal Reserve raised interest rates at the fastest pace since the 1980s, from near 0% to 5.25–5.5% by 2023. The market constantly worried that rapid rate hikes would push the economy into recession, but the actual outcome was a soft landing — inflation receded, and the job market remained strong.

In 2025, US GDP growth reached 2%, and inflation plateaued — tariffs pushed up commodity prices, while services inflation decreased, with the two offsetting each other. The fiscal deficit slightly narrowed from 6.3% of GDP to 5.9%, but total government debt rose to 123.9% of GDP.

New Variable in 2025: Stagflationary Pressure from Tariffs

The Trump administration's large-scale tariff policy was the biggest exogenous variable in 2025. Tariffs are a typical cost-push inflation: rising import costs → shrinking denominator → rising prices. This is unrelated to domestic credit expansion, and interest rate hikes are not the right remedy, thus putting the Federal Reserve in a dilemma.

The CBO predicts real GDP growth of approximately 1.4% in 2025, lower than previous expectations, primarily dragged by tariff shocks; growth is expected to rebound to 2.2% in 2026, partly due to the short-term stimulus effect of tax cuts.

The IMF warns that current interest rates are already close to neutral, with almost no room for rate cuts in 2026, especially considering the upside risk of energy prices.

Judging from Dalio's Framework Position

The US is in a dual position of the late stage of the short-term debt cycle superimposed with the accumulation of long-term debt cycle pressure. The soft landing is real, but the debt-to-GDP ratio continues to rise, and fiscal deficits remain high, thinning the long-term debt cycle's buffer.

Japan: Historic Turning Point, Yet Foundation Remains Unstable

Bank of Japan Finally Begins Raising Interest Rates

The Bank of Japan raised its benchmark interest rate to 0.75% in December 2025, a 30-year high. Inflation has exceeded the 2% target for 44 consecutive months. This is the first time in three decades that the BOJ has implemented continuous interest rate hikes under real inflationary pressure, potentially marking a historic turning point from a long era of deflation.

But Real Wages Remain Negative

Nominal contractual income growth accelerated to 2.2% in November 2025, but inflation-adjusted real wages still decreased by 2.8% year-on-year in November 2025. Nominal wages are rising, but inflation is running faster, and consumers' real purchasing power is still being eroded.

This reveals a key issue: a significant proportion of Japan's current inflation comes from temporary cost-push factors like rising food prices, rather than the demand-driven inflation the central bank hopes to see. Among the two core inflation indicators, only the trimmed mean inflation barely exceeds the 2% target, and the BOJ itself admits that core inflation is still slowly approaching the 2% target.

The BOJ's Dilemma

The Bank of Japan maintained interest rates at 0.75% at its March 2026 meeting, with rhetoric indicating that uncertainties from the Middle East situation influenced the decision, and warned that rising oil prices could temporarily push up inflation while suppressing growth.

Wanting to raise rates but not daring to rush — this accurately describes the BOJ's current predicament: monetary policy normalization is the goal, but energy shocks, negative real wages, and uncertain external demand are all constraints.

ING predicts the BOJ's next rate hike will be no earlier than October 2026; however, if the yen continues to depreciate and push up import prices, the timeline could be brought forward to the second quarter.

China: Deleveraging Underway, Systemic Deflationary Pressure Exists

Surface Figures and Structural Divergence

China's GDP growth in 2025 was approximately 5%, seemingly meeting the target, but the growth structure was severely imbalanced: exports and industrial production remained strong, while consumption and private investment remained sluggish, and fixed asset investment actually contracted.

Notably, one of the most important policy choices in 2025 was Beijing's decision not to pursue the old path of large-scale debt-driven stimulus, but instead to focus on manufacturing upgrades, green industries, and technological self-reliance.

Deflation is Already a Systemic Reality

The GDP deflator, which measures the overall price level, has been in continuous negative growth since 2023. China is facing a sustained deflation extremely rare among major economies since World War II, with the only comparable historical precedent being Japan's lost decades.

In Dalio's framework, this is a dangerous signal. The deflation transmission chain:

Asset prices fall (housing) → Household wealth shrinks (nominal terms) → Consumption willingness decreases → Corporate pricing power weakens → Prices fall → Deflationary expectations form → Consumption further postponed → Spiral strengthens

Monetary Policy Transmission Breakdown

The People's Bank of China is easing monetary conditions, but the average actual lending rate has only decreased by about 4 basis points, with almost no substantial change. Against the backdrop of deflation and continuously falling producer prices, real interest rates are actually relatively high, further suppressing new investment willingness.

This is highly similar to the core symptoms of Japan in the 1990s — monetary policy transmission broke down at the corporate borrowing willingness link.

Will China Follow Japan's Path?

The IMF explicitly points out that China's domestic demand remains weak, partly due to an unstable real estate market, which suppresses consumer confidence and creates deflationary pressure. The IMF recommends adopting more forceful expansionary policies and advancing social security system reforms to reduce residents' precautionary saving motives.

Similarities: Asset bubble burst (real estate), excessive debt, insufficient demand, deflationary pressure, aging population beginning.

Differences: The Chinese government's direct control over the economy is much stronger than Japan's, with more diverse policy tools; urbanization rate still has some room for improvement; however, the problem of "getting old before getting rich" is more severe than Japan's back then, and the window for policy adjustment is narrower.

7.4 Middle East Conflict (2026): Exogenous Shock Superimposed on Fragile Foundations

Event Background

On February 28, 2026, Israel and the United States launched military strikes against Iran, after which Iran blockaded the Strait of Hormuz, cutting off approximately 20% of global oil trade routes. The International Energy Agency characterized this as the largest global energy supply disruption in history.

On April 8, the US and Iran reached a two-week ceasefire agreement, brokered by Pakistan. The ceasefire framework required an immediate cessation of hostilities, reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, and entering a 15 to 20-day negotiation period. Both sides have violated the ceasefire since its inception.

As of April 20, the ceasefire is set to expire on April 21, and a new round of negotiations is still underway, with the outcome undetermined.

Positioning within the Framework

This is an extreme example of an "exogenous supply-side shock."

The Hormuz blockade directly compresses the denominator in the price equation:

Sharp decrease in energy supply → Oil and LNG prices surge → Global production costs rise across the board (transportation, chemicals, electricity, agriculture) → Prices rise but without increased demand → Real purchasing power declines → Growth slows

Result: Stagflation risk rises across the board, central bank tools have limited effectiveness.

The IMF's March 2026 assessment indicated that regardless of how the conflict evolves, all paths point to higher prices and slower growth. Energy is the main transmission channel, and for energy-importing countries, the effect is equivalent to imposing a huge hidden tax on all residents.

Asymmetry of Impact

The three major economies experienced vastly different degrees of impact:

Approximately 90% of Japan's crude oil imports come from the Middle East, with most passing through the Strait of Hormuz. South Korea imports about 70% of its crude oil from the Middle East, and over 95% passes through the strait. LNG prices have surged significantly in Asian markets, and South Korea has launched a market stabilization plan of approximately $6.8 billion.

China possesses the world's largest onshore crude oil reserves, approximately 1.2 billion barrels as of January 2026, which can cover about 108 days of import demand at current refining rates. Furthermore, Iran reportedly allows Chinese vessels to pass through the strait, and both sides are discussing allowing goods to pass with RMB settlement.

Core Uncertainties

Analysts estimate that if the situation develops towards a continued ceasefire and advancing negotiations, oil prices will gradually fall back to around $70 by year-end; if the conflict restarts, Brent crude could return above $100; if Iran attacks Gulf energy infrastructure, oil prices could break above $130 in extreme scenarios.

The duration of the conflict is the core variable in all analyses, and its ultimate direction cannot be determined at present.

7.5 Discrepancy Between Macro Data and Daily Perception

This is the most common cognitive confusion when understanding macroeconomics: everything seems normal, even thriving, around us, yet macroeconomic data describes severe pressures. The two are not contradictory; they are different facets of the same reality.

Cross-Section vs. Trend

Daily perception sees a cross-section: the consumption behavior and mental state of these people, at this moment, in this place.

Macroeconomic forecasting looks at trends: the direction of change in wage growth, debt burden, demographic structure, and asset prices over a scale of years to decades.

Macroeconomic deterioration is often not as suddenly visible as a crisis; it's more like a frog in slowly boiling water — each day is manageable, but looking back ten years later, living standards have quietly declined. For most of Japan's lost three decades, Tokyo's cafes were full, and young people were out shopping.

Structural Reasons for Japanese Youth's Active Consumption

Japanese youth pursue fashion and consume actively, backed by real structural logic:

Not buying homes, not buying cars, not getting married, not having children (these large expenditures are systematically contracting) → Money saved is instead used for immediate enjoyment: dining, travel, fashion, concerts

This is rational intertemporal substitution — since the future doesn't look better than the present, and long-term asset (real estate) return expectations are low, it's better to concentrate spending on consumption that can be enjoyed now. This is not a sign of "being rich," but a rational adaptation to long-term pessimistic expectations.

From the perspective of nominal vs. real values: young people's nominal consumption may be increasing, but real wages are negative, and real savings rates are declining — consumption appears active on paper, but purchasing power is actually being eroded.

Selection Bias

In Japan's Tokyo metropolitan area, well-educated young people with stable jobs belong to a relatively secure class. Their situation does not represent:

  • The actual situation of local economies (continuous population outflow, business contraction)
  • The condition of small manufacturing towns
  • The living pressure of the informally employed population (approximately 40% of Japan's labor force)

Macro data captures the whole, while daily observations are limited by the homogeneity of social circles. The superposition of the two easily leads to systematic over-optimism bias.

A Useful Habit for Verification

Whenever your intuition tells you "things feel pretty good," you can check against the following questions:

  • Are real wages (nominal wage growth - inflation rate) rising or falling?
  • Where does the debt burden (debt repayment amount / income) stand historically?
  • Is the consumption you see supported by income, or by reduced savings or borrowing?

The answers to these three questions are often closer to macroeconomic reality than sensory impressions.
